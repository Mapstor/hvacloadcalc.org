# FAQ
# ============================================================
faq:
  - q: "What R-value should my attic insulation be?"
    a: |
      DOE recommends R-30 to R-49 for warmer climates (zones 1-3) and R-49 to R-60 for cooler
      climates (zones 4-8) for existing homes. New construction code under 2021 IECC is R-49
      or R-60 in most zones. The exact recommended value for your home depends on your climate
      zone, current insulation level, and energy goals.

  - q: "How thick is R-38 attic insulation?"
    a: |
      Depends on the material. R-38 in fiberglass batts is about 10.5 inches thick. R-38 in
      loose-fill fiberglass is about 16.5 inches. R-38 in cellulose is about 10.5 inches.
      R-38 in closed-cell spray foam is about 5.5 inches. R-38 in open-cell spray foam is
      about 10.5 inches. Always verify with the product's labeled R-value per inch.

  - q: "How do I measure my existing attic R-value?"
    a: |
      Three steps: (1) Measure the depth of insulation in inches with a tape measure or ruler.
      Take multiple measurements; settled or compressed areas read lower. (2) Identify the
      insulation type by appearance (fiberglass batts pink/yellow, loose fiberglass white,
      cellulose gray-brown, spray foam yellow/peach). (3) Multiply depth by the R-value per
      inch for that type (fiberglass batt ~3.0/in, loose fiberglass ~2.3/in, cellulose ~3.6/in,
      open-cell foam ~3.6/in, closed-cell foam ~6.5/in).

  - q: "Is R-30 enough attic insulation?"
    a: |
      R-30 is the lower end of DOE's recommendation for the warmest US climates (zones 1-2).
      For zone 3 and above, R-30 is below recommended. For new construction, 2021 IECC requires
      R-49 in zones 2-4 and R-60 in zones 5-8. Whether R-30 is "enough" for an existing home
      depends on climate, fuel cost, and your goals.

  - q: "Should I just add more insulation on top of what I have?"
    a: |
      Usually yes — adding insulation on top of existing insulation (called "topping off") is
      cost-effective if the existing insulation is in good condition and the attic is air-sealed.
      Two cautions: (1) ensure the existing insulation isn't wet, moldy, or compressed (which
      would warrant removal); (2) air seal first — adding insulation over leaky areas wastes
      money. The new layer's R-value adds to the existing layer's R-value.

  - q: "What's better for attic insulation: fiberglass or cellulose?"
    a: |
      Both work well when installed correctly. Cellulose is denser (R-3.6/in vs R-2.3/in for
      loose fiberglass), settles less in the long run, and resists air movement slightly better.
      Fiberglass is lighter, doesn't absorb water (cellulose does, though it dries out), and is
      cheaper per inch. For most attic retrofits, cellulose performs slightly better in
      real-world conditions. For batts between joists, fiberglass is more common.

  - q: "Does spray foam in the attic eliminate the need for venting?"
    a: |
      It can, when the spray foam is applied to the underside of the roof deck instead of the
      attic floor. This creates an "unvented" or "conditioned" attic — the attic becomes part
      of the conditioned space rather than vented to outdoors. This approach has design
      considerations (HVAC equipment must be appropriate for the new conditioned space,
      moisture handling matters, building code varies by jurisdiction). It's a different
      strategy than topping off existing insulation on the attic floor.

  - q: "What is R-value, exactly?"
    a: |
      R-value is a material's resistance to conductive heat flow, measured in (ft²·°F·h)/BTU
      in the US. Higher R-value means more resistance, less heat flow. A material's R-value
      depends on its conductive properties, density, and thickness. R-value is tested under
      ASTM C518 and labeled per FTC R-Value Rule (16 CFR 460). It does not account for air
      leakage or radiant heat transfer separately, which is why air sealing is critical.

  - q: "Does insulation R-value decrease over time?"
    a: |
      Most insulation types maintain rated R-value indefinitely if kept dry and undisturbed.
      Two exceptions: (1) Closed-cell spray foam gradually loses some R-value over years as
      blowing agents diffuse out (rated R-value typically accounts for this — "aged R-value"
      is what's labeled). (2) Any insulation that gets wet loses R-value temporarily;
      fiberglass and mineral wool recover; cellulose can recover but is more vulnerable to
      mold. Settling is a separate issue — loose-fill insulation can settle 5-20% over time,
      reducing thickness and thus R-value.

  - q: "Is more insulation always better?"
    a: |
      Up to a point, yes — but there's a diminishing-returns curve. Each additional R-unit
      saves less energy than the previous one (going from R-19 to R-38 saves a lot; going
      from R-49 to R-60 saves less). Most homes hit the cost-effectiveness threshold somewhere
      between R-38 and R-60 depending on climate, fuel cost, and labor cost. Beyond a certain
      R-value, the marginal cost exceeds the marginal savings, and money is better spent on
      windows, air sealing, or other envelope upgrades.

## Search intent

"Attic R-value" reflects three reader cohorts:

1. **Researching homeowner before upgrade** (~50%): considering adding attic insulation, wants to know how much and what kind. Common search variations: "what r value for attic," "recommended attic r value," "attic r value by climate zone."

2. **Contractor / HVAC professional reference** (~20%): looking up code-minimum or recommended values to spec a project. Searches like "iecc attic r value" and "department of energy attic insulation."

3. **DIYer measuring or improving existing insulation** (~30%): wants to know current R-value of their attic, how to measure it, which type to use. Search variations include "how to measure attic r value," "attic r value calculator," type-specific terms like "r38 attic insulation" and "fiberglass vs cellulose attic."

The article structures as: what R-value is (definition for everyone), why attic R-value matters (HVAC sizing, comfort, cost), recommendations by climate zone (the highest-traffic intent), how to measure existing, insulation types and material-specific R-per-inch, air sealing as the critical prerequisite, payback economics, beyond the attic.

This article is the building science insulation cornerstone — child pages will cover wall R-value, basement, types (fiberglass vs cellulose vs foam), air sealing, by-zone tables, and measurement. This article links to those for depth.

### H2 3 — How Attic Insulation Works
**Target: 250 words**

## Technical depth specification

### Section 1 — R-value definition and units

R-value = ΔT / (Q × A)
where ΔT is the temperature difference across the material, Q is the heat flow rate, A is the area.

US units: (ft² × °F × hr) / BTU
SI units: (m² × K) / W

Conversion: 1 (ft² × °F × hr) / BTU = 0.176 (m² × K) / W

So a US R-30 attic insulation = SI R-5.3 (or RSI-5.3).

Note: R-value adds linearly when layers are stacked. R-19 batt + R-19 batt added on top = R-38 total (assuming both are installed properly with no gaps).

### Section 7 — air leakage physics

Air leakage carries enthalpy (heat + moisture). One ACH50 typically translates to ~5% natural ACH at typical pressure differences. A poorly air-sealed attic can have leakage rates 3-5× higher than a well-sealed one.

Effective R-value reduction from air leakage isn't trivially calculable but field studies (Lawrence Berkeley Lab, RDH Building Science) consistently show 30-50% effective R-value reduction in attics with code-level insulation but no specific air sealing measures.
