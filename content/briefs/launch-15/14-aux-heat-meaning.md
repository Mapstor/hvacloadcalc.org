# FAQ

faq:
  - q: "What does aux heat mean on a thermostat?"
    a: |
      Aux heat (short for auxiliary heat) means electric resistance backup heat is running
      to supplement your heat pump. It typically fires when outdoor temperatures fall
      below the heat pump's balance point, during defrost cycles, or when the thermostat
      is recovering from a setback. It's normal operation in cold weather, not a malfunction.

  - q: "Is aux heat the same as emergency heat?"
    a: |
      No. Aux heat is automatic and runs alongside the heat pump as a helper. Emergency
      heat is manually selected and shuts off the heat pump entirely, leaving only the
      electric resistance strips to heat your home. Aux heat is for normal cold weather;
      emergency heat is for when the heat pump has failed or in extreme conditions.

  - q: "Why is aux heat on when it's not that cold outside?"
    a: |
      Common reasons: (1) thermostat is recovering from a setback (you raised the temperature
      3°F or more, and the system is using aux to catch up faster); (2) a heat pump defrost
      cycle is in progress (aux runs briefly to maintain comfort while the outdoor coil
      melts ice); (3) thermostat is misconfigured (aux threshold set too aggressively);
      (4) the heat pump itself is struggling (low refrigerant, dirty coil, undersized).

  - q: "Is aux heat expensive?"
    a: |
      Yes, relatively. Aux heat is electric resistance heating with COP = 1 — every watt
      of electricity becomes one watt of heat. The heat pump operates at COP 2-4 in
      heating mode, so the same heat costs 2-4× less. Brief aux heat during defrost or
      cold snaps is fine; constant aux heat indicates a problem that's costing real money.

  - q: "Should aux heat always be on in winter?"
    a: |
      No. Aux heat should fire only when the heat pump can't keep up alone — typically
      below the balance point (around 25-35°F for standard heat pumps, lower for
      cold-climate models). If aux is on continuously even in mild winter weather (above
      40°F), the system is misconfigured or the heat pump is failing.

  - q: "How do I know if aux heat is on?"
    a: |
      Look at your thermostat display. Most smart thermostats show an "Aux Heat,"
      "Auxiliary Heat," or "AUX" indicator when aux is active. Many also show this in their
      mobile app's history or energy reports. Older thermostats may just show a light or
      small icon. If you have an electric meter, you can also tell by the spike in usage
      when aux fires (3-10× higher than heat pump alone).

  - q: "Will aux heat damage my heat pump?"
    a: |
      No. Aux heat is part of the system design and is intended to help during cold weather.
      It doesn't damage the heat pump in any way. The cost (electricity) is the only
      downside of running aux. Damage concerns come from running the heat pump in cold
      weather without aux backup (compressor can be stressed), not from running aux itself.

  - q: "Can I turn aux heat off completely?"
    a: |
      You can on most thermostats, but it's usually a bad idea. Aux heat is there as
      protection: if your heat pump fails, gets restricted, or just can't keep up on a
      record cold day, aux heat prevents your home from freezing. Some smart thermostats
      offer "heat pump only" or "no aux" modes for energy savings, but they usually allow
      aux for emergencies. Verify your specific thermostat's behavior before disabling.

  - q: "Why is aux heat called 'auxiliary' or 'second stage'?"
    a: |
      The terms refer to the same thing. "Auxiliary" emphasizes the helper role. "Second
      stage" or "Stage 2 heat" emphasizes the staging order — the heat pump is stage 1,
      aux is stage 2. Some thermostats use one term, some the other, some both. Same
      hardware (electric resistance strips downstream of the indoor coil), same function.

  - q: "Is my thermostat showing aux heat because the heat pump is broken?"
    a: |
      Not necessarily. Aux heat firing is normal in cold weather and during defrost. It
      could indicate a problem if: (1) aux is on continuously in warm winter weather, (2)
      the outdoor unit isn't running at all when aux is on (and you're not in emergency
      heat mode), (3) the heat pump runs but aux always fires alongside even in mild
      weather. Use the troubleshooting decision tree in the article to diagnose.

### H2 1 — What Aux Heat Is (Direct Answer)
**Target: 250 words**

- Open with prose anchor (direct definition in first sentence)
- Aux heat = auxiliary heat = electric resistance backup heat
- Found in most US heat pump installations
- It's automatic and runs alongside your heat pump
- It is NORMAL operation, not a malfunction
- Different from emergency heat (link forward to section 6)
- Link to /heat-pump/aux-heat/

### H2 2 — When Aux Heat Fires
**Target: 300 words**

- Reference SVG: aux-heat-when-it-fires
- Three main triggers:
  1. **Below the balance point**: outdoor temperature drops below the heat pump's capacity threshold (typically 25-35°F for standard heat pumps)
  2. **During defrost cycles**: outdoor coil melts ice every 30-90 minutes in cold humid weather; aux maintains indoor comfort during the 5-15 minutes of defrost
  3. **During thermostat setback recovery**: when you raise setpoint 3°F or more, many smart thermostats fire aux to reach the new setpoint faster
- The balance point varies by heat pump model and home insulation
- Cold-climate heat pumps shift balance points significantly lower
- Link to /heat-pump/cold-climate/balance-point/, /heat-pump/cold-climate/defrost-cycle/

### H2 3 — What You See on the Display
**Target: 250 words**

- Reference SVG: aux-heat-by-thermostat-brand

  - **Ecobee**: shows "Auxiliary Heat" with thermometer icon, visible in HomeIQ reports
  - **Honeywell**: shows "Aux Heat" or "AUX" with red indicator (model-dependent: T6, T9, Vision Pro)
- Older thermostats: simple light or icon
- Some thermostats show "Stage 2" or "Heat 2" for the same thing
- Mobile app reporting (most smart thermostats track aux runtime over time)

### H2 4 — How Much Does Aux Heat Cost?
**Target: 300 words**

- Reference SVG: aux-heat-cost-vs-heat-pump
- Aux heat is electric resistance: COP = 1.0
- Heat pump heating: COP 2-4 depending on conditions
- So aux heat is 2-4× more expensive per BTU of heating delivered
- Concrete example: 10kW heating load for 1 hour at $0.16/kWh
  - Heat pump COP 2.5: ~$0.40/hour for that 25,000 BTU/hr
  - Aux heat: $1.60/hour for 10,000 BTU/hr (less heating, same cost)
- Brief aux during defrost (10-15 min) costs cents
- Hours of continuous aux on cold days can add up to $50-100/month

### H2 5 — Is Aux Heat a Problem?
**Target: 250 words**

- Usually no. Aux heat fires because:
  - It's cold (normal)
  - There's a defrost cycle (normal)
  - You raised the setpoint (normal recovery)
- Aux heat is bad when:
  - Continuous in mild winter weather (above 40°F)
  - Always on whenever the heat pump runs
  - Outdoor unit is OFF but you're not in Em Heat mode (different problem)
- Aux heat doesn't damage the heat pump
- Aux heat costs real money but it's also doing real work — heating your home in conditions where the heat pump alone can't keep up
- The decision: investigate if aux heat seems out of proportion to conditions

### H2 6 — Aux Heat vs Emergency Heat
**Target: 250 words**

- Reference SVG: aux-vs-emergency-comparison
- **Aux heat** = automatic, helper, runs alongside heat pump
- **Emergency heat (Em Heat)** = manual, substitute, heat pump is OFF
- Same hardware (electric resistance strips), different control logic
- When to use Em Heat:
  - Heat pump is failed or broken
  - Extreme cold (below the heat pump's operating range)
  - Outdoor unit is iced over or damaged
  - You manually want to bypass the heat pump
- When NOT to use Em Heat:
  - Normal cold weather (let aux heat work automatically)
  - Saving money (Em Heat is MORE expensive than aux + heat pump)
  - As a default mode (you'll waste a lot of electricity)
- Link to /heat-pump/aux-heat/vs-emergency-heat/

### H2 7 — When to Worry and What to Do
**Target: 400 words**

  - Is it cold outside? → Normal
  - Setback recovery? → Normal

- Specific scenarios:
  - **Aux on continuously in mild weather**: thermostat probably misconfigured, or heat pump is failing

## Technical depth specification

### Section 2 — balance point quick math

Balance point = outdoor temperature where heat pump capacity equals home heating load.

Standard heat pump example:
- Home design heating load: 45,000 BTU/hr at 5°F (heating design temperature)
- Heat pump capacity: 36,000 BTU/hr at 47°F, drops to 22,000 at 17°F, 12,000 at 5°F
- Linear interpolation finds balance point ≈ 28°F
- Above 28°F: heat pump alone handles load
- Below 28°F: aux fills gap (e.g., at 5°F, aux provides 45,000 - 12,000 = 33,000 BTU/hr)

Cold-climate heat pump shifts balance point:
- Same home but with 30,000 BTU/hr capacity at 17°F
- Balance point shifts to ~12°F
- Aux runtime drops dramatically

### Section 4 — cost example math

10kW (10,000W = 10 kilowatts) heating delivered to indoor air for 1 hour:

Heat pump scenario:
- Electric input: 10,000 / COP_pump = 10,000 / 2.5 = 4,000 W = 4 kWh per hour
- At $0.16/kWh: $0.64/hour
- Wait — let me redo this. 10 kW input × COP 2.5 = 25 kW output (heating).

Cleaner framing:
- Heat pump consuming 4 kW electricity at COP 2.5 → 10 kW heating output. Cost: 4 kWh × $0.16 = $0.64.
- Aux heat consuming 10 kW electricity at COP 1.0 → 10 kW heating output. Cost: 10 kWh × $0.16 = $1.60.

Same 10 kW heat output, aux costs 2.5× more (matches COP ratio).

Brief aux during defrost (say 8 minutes per cycle, 4 cycles per cold humid evening):
- ~32 minutes of aux per night
- 32/60 × 10 kW × $0.16 = $0.85/night in cold weather

Continuous aux (8 hours overnight, severe cold day):
- 8 × 10 kW × $0.16 = $12.80 for that one night
- Across a heating season with many such nights: $50-100/month aux costs

### Section 7 — diagnostic specifics

Aux heat threshold settings on smart thermostats:
- Nest: "Auxiliary heat lockout" setting in Equipment menu; default temperature varies but typically engages aux when heat pump can't keep up
- Ecobee: HVAC settings → Heat Pump → Auxiliary Heat Temperature; default 35°F outdoor lockout
- Honeywell: model-dependent; T9 and Vision Pro have outdoor temperature aux lockouts

Common misconfigurations:
- Aux threshold set too high (e.g., aux fires below 50°F instead of 35°F): wastes energy
- Aux threshold set too low or disabled: home can't maintain setpoint on cold days
- "Heat Pump Balance" feature on some Nest models: automatically tunes aux usage; may need to be enabled

> Aux heat on your thermostat means auxiliary heat — electric resistance backup heat that supplements your heat pump. When you see "Aux Heat," "Auxiliary Heat," "AUX," or "Stage 2 Heat" on your thermostat, it means electric strip heaters are running alongside the heat pump to help warm your home.
