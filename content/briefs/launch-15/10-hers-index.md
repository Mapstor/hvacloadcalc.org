# FAQ
# ============================================================
faq:
  - q: "What is a good HERS Index score?"
    a: |
      For new construction in 2024, HERS 60-70 is typical for code-built homes; HERS 50-60
      is typical for ENERGY STAR certified new homes; HERS 30-40 is required for DOE Zero
      Energy Ready Home certification; HERS 0 is net-zero (the home produces as much energy
      as it consumes, typically through solar PV). For existing homes, the median is around
      HERS 130. "Good" depends on context: a 1985 home brought down to HERS 80 is a notable
      improvement; a new 2024 home at HERS 80 is below average.

  - q: "How is the HERS Index calculated?"
    a: |
      A RESNET-certified rater inspects the home, conducts diagnostic testing (blower door
      for air leakage, duct blaster for duct leakage), and inputs all data into approved
      software (REM/Rate, Ekotrope, or others). The software models the home's annual energy
      consumption and compares it to a "reference home" — a hypothetical version of the same
      home built to 2006 IECC code. The HERS Index is the ratio of the rated home's energy
      use to the reference home's energy use, multiplied by 100.

  - q: "What does a HERS Index of 0 mean?"
    a: |
      A HERS Index of 0 means the home produces as much energy as it consumes on an annual
      net basis. This is usually achieved through a combination of high-performance envelope
      (insulation, air sealing, windows), high-efficiency equipment (heat pump, heat pump
      water heater), and on-site renewable energy generation (typically solar PV). HERS 0
      is the definition of a "net-zero" home.

  - q: "How much does a HERS rating cost?"
    a: |
      For new construction, a HERS rating typically costs $400-1,000, often paid by the
      builder and included in the home price. For existing homes, ratings cost more
      ($500-1,500) because the rater must investigate and verify existing construction
      details. Some utilities subsidize HERS ratings for energy efficiency programs.

  - q: "Is HERS the same as ENERGY STAR?"
    a: |
      No. HERS is a numeric score (0 to 200+). ENERGY STAR is a pass/fail certification
      that uses HERS as one criterion among several. To be ENERGY STAR Certified New Home,
      the home must score below a certain HERS threshold (varies by climate zone, typically
      HERS 50-60) AND meet additional requirements for ducts, mechanical equipment, lighting,
      and quality installation.

  - q: "Can my HERS Index be used for federal tax credits?"
    a: |
      Yes, in specific contexts. The 45L New Energy Efficient Home Credit (up to $5,000 per
      unit) requires HERS-based certification at specific thresholds. The 25C Energy
      Efficient Home Improvement Credit doesn't directly use HERS but uses equipment
      efficiency criteria that contribute to lower HERS scores. State and utility programs
      often tie incentives to specific HERS thresholds.

  - q: "Can I get a HERS Index for an existing home?"
    a: |
      Yes. Existing-home HERS ratings are less common than new construction ratings but
      provide useful baseline data for renovation planning, refinancing (some lenders offer
      energy-efficient mortgages with HERS data), or before/after improvement comparisons.
      The methodology is the same as new construction.

  - q: "How can I lower my HERS Index?"
    a: |
      Most impactful improvements, in rough order: (1) reduce air leakage through air sealing,
      (2) increase attic insulation, (3) upgrade windows (especially if currently single-pane),
      (4) replace older HVAC equipment with high-efficiency models (heat pumps typically score
      best), (5) install heat pump water heater, (6) add solar PV if going for very low HERS.
      Each improvement adds, but the cost-effectiveness varies dramatically by climate, current
      condition, and equipment age.

  - q: "Is HERS used for code compliance?"
    a: |
      Yes, as one of three pathways under 2021 IECC. The Energy Rating Index (ERI) pathway
      (essentially HERS) requires the home to score at or below a specific ERI for the climate
      zone. The other pathways are prescriptive (specific component requirements) and
      performance (whole-home energy modeling). Many builders use the ERI/HERS pathway because
      it provides flexibility on individual component specifications.

  - q: "Does a lower HERS Index mean lower energy bills?"
    a: |
      Generally yes, but the relationship isn't linear and depends on local energy costs.
      A home with HERS 50 uses approximately half the energy of the 2006 reference home;
      with HERS 75, three-quarters. Actual energy bills depend on local rates, occupant
      behavior, weather, and equipment maintenance, but HERS scores correlate strongly with
      energy bills across comparable homes.

## Search intent

Three cohorts:

1. **New-home buyer or builder reviewer** (~50%): saw HERS on a marketing brochure or new-home spec sheet, wants to know what the number actually means and whether it's good.

2. **Energy efficiency improver** (~30%): considering an existing-home rating or improvements; understands HERS exists but wants to know how to use it for decision-making.

3. **Tax-credit / incentive researcher** (~20%): figured out HERS is tied to credits and rebates, wants to know the specific thresholds and processes.

Article structures to: definition (everyone), how to read the scale + counter-intuitive direction (everyone), how it's calculated (cohort 2 + 3), distribution / what's typical (everyone), how to lower the score (cohort 2), HERS vs ENERGY STAR vs ZERH (everyone), tax credits and incentives (cohort 3), getting a rating (cohort 2 + 3).

The article does NOT recommend specific raters or services (RESNET maintains a finder; we link there). It teaches readers to interpret HERS and know what they're getting before spending money on a rating.

### H2 1 — What HERS Is
**Target: 300 words**

- Open with prose anchor
- HERS = Home Energy Rating System
- HERS Index = numeric output, 0 to 200+
- Developed and maintained by RESNET (Residential Energy Services Network)
- Standardized under ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301
- Used for: code compliance (IECC ERI pathway), tax credits, certifications, energy-efficient mortgages, before/after improvement comparisons
- Reference SVG: hers-vs-mpg-analogy
- Link to /building-science/ and to external RESNET

### H2 2 — How to Read the Score
**Target: 250 words**

- 0 = net-zero (home produces as much energy as it consumes)
- 100 = 2006 IECC reference home (the baseline)
- Higher than 100 = less efficient than 2006 reference
- Each point = approximately 1% energy difference
- HERS 70 = 30% less energy than reference; HERS 50 = 50% less; HERS 130 = 30% more
- Lower is always better
- Link to /building-science/hers-index/scale/

### H2 3 — Quick HERS Estimate
**Target: 250 words**

- The site's HERS estimator gives a planning-grade approximation
- Inputs: location (climate zone), square footage, insulation level, window quality, HVAC equipment type, air sealing estimate, water heater type
- Outputs: approximate HERS range and improvement suggestions
- Caveat: not a certified rating; for that you need a RESNET-certified rater (process described in section 8)
- Use cases: planning improvements, evaluating new home listings, before paying for full rating
- Link to /tools/hers-index-estimator/

### H2 4 — How the Calculation Works
**Target: 350 words**

- The rated home: the actual home being measured
- The reference home: a hypothetical version of the same physical home built exactly to 2006 IECC code
  - Same orientation, geometry, square footage
  - 2006 IECC envelope (R-values, U-factors, infiltration)
  - 2006 IECC mechanical equipment (typically AFUE 78% furnace, SEER 13 AC, 0.59 EF water heater)
- The software (REM/Rate, Ekotrope, others) models annual energy use for both:
  - Heating (per Manual J load and equipment efficiency)
  - Cooling
  - Water heating
  - Lighting (occupant behavior assumed)
  - Plug loads (standard occupancy assumed)
- HERS Index = (rated home annual energy / reference home annual energy) × 100
- On-site renewable generation credited (PV reduces score)

### H2 6 — How to Lower a HERS Score
**Target: 350 words**

- Reference SVG: hers-reduction-stack
- Improvements in rough order of cost-effectiveness:
  1. **Air sealing** (the single most cost-effective improvement; ~5-10 HERS points)
  2. **Attic insulation top-off** (3-8 HERS points)
  3. **HVAC upgrade** (especially heat pump replacing AC + furnace, can be 10-15 HERS points)
  4. **Water heater upgrade** (heat pump water heater, ~3-5 HERS points)
  5. **Window upgrade** (if single-pane to high-performance, can be 5-10 HERS points; otherwise smaller)
  6. **Solar PV** (the only way to reach low HERS without massive envelope work)
- The diminishing returns: HERS 100 → 70 is much cheaper than HERS 50 → 30
- Each improvement's HERS impact depends on climate, current conditions, equipment age

## Technical depth specification

### Section 4 — methodology specifics

The HERS Index calculation follows ANSI/RESNET/ICC 301. Key elements:

**Reference home definition**: per 2006 IECC, with:
- Air leakage: 7 ACH50 for existing homes, varying by climate zone
- Envelope: 2006 IECC prescriptive values
- Equipment: 78% AFUE gas furnace OR 7.7 HSPF heat pump, 13 SEER AC, 0.59 EF gas water heater OR 0.95 EF electric (specifics vary by climate)
- Window U-factor: per 2006 IECC
- Lighting and appliance load profiles: standardized

**Rated home modeling**: actual home characteristics inputted to approved software:
- REM/Rate (Architectural Energy Corporation)
- Ekotrope RATER (Ekotrope, Inc.)
- Others certified by RESNET

**Energy calculation**: annual purchased energy in kBtu, weighted by:
- Heating, cooling, water heating, lighting, large appliances, MELS (miscellaneous electric loads)
- Site-to-source energy conversion for fuel switching analysis

**On-site renewables credit**: PV generation modeled per PVWatts or similar, credited against consumption (annual net basis).

**Score formula**: `HERS = (Total Annual Energy Use of Rated Home / Total Annual Energy Use of Reference Home) × 100`

### Section 5 — distribution data

RESNET publishes annual statistics from rated home registry:
- 2024 new construction HERS median: ~62
- 2024 new construction HERS at 25th-75th percentile: ~52-72
- ENERGY STAR Certified New Homes 2024: ~55 median
- DOE ZERH 2024: ~38 median
- Existing-home registry (smaller dataset, biased toward improvements): median ~95 but unrepresentative

US Census American Housing Survey data on existing housing stock (extrapolated to HERS-equivalent values): median ~130.

### Section 7 — incentive specifics (current as of 2024)

**45L New Energy Efficient Home Credit** (effective 2023-2032):
- ENERGY STAR Certified New Home: $2,500/unit (single-family) or $500 (multifamily)
- DOE Zero Energy Ready Home: $5,000/unit (single-family) or $1,000 (multifamily)
- Multifamily bonuses for prevailing wage compliance

**25C Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit** (effective 2023-2032):
- Up to $3,200/year for qualifying improvements
- Heat pump: 30% of cost up to $2,000
- Insulation/air sealing: 30% of cost up to $1,200
- Windows/skylights: 30% of cost up to $600
- Doors: 30% of cost up to $500

State and utility programs vary; check local programs.

## Prose anchors (voice calibration)

### Opening hook (use exactly, do not rewrite)

> The HERS Index is a number from 0 to 200-plus that tells you how energy-efficient a home is. Lower is better. The scale is calibrated so 100 represents a reference home built exactly to 2006 IECC code, 0 represents a net-zero home that produces as much energy as it consumes, and scores above 100 represent homes less efficient than the 2006 reference.
>
> The number alone is almost useless without context. A new home at HERS 65 is below the certification threshold for ENERGY STAR. A 1985 home brought down to HERS 65 through retrofits is a remarkable achievement. The same number means different things depending on the starting point.
>
> This article explains what HERS is, how to read the scale, how a HERS rating is actually calculated, what scores are typical for new and existing homes, how to lower a score, how HERS relates to ENERGY STAR and Zero Energy Ready Home certifications, and what a rating costs.

### Anchor paragraph — H2 section 6 (Lowering the score), opening (use exactly)

> The cheapest HERS points come from sealing air leaks. The most expensive come from solar panels. Between those two extremes, the cost per HERS point varies dramatically with climate, current condition, and equipment age.
