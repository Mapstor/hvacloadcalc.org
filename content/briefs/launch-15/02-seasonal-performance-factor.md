faq:
  - q: "What is a good SPF for a heat pump?"
    a: |
      An SPF of 3.0 means a heat pump delivers 3 units of heat for every unit of electricity consumed.
      In mild climates (zones 3-4), well-installed heat pumps routinely achieve SPF 3.0 or higher.
      In cold climates (zones 5-7), conventional heat pumps may drop to SPF 2.0-2.5 because of more
      aux heat usage and lower compressor COP at design temperatures; cold-climate models with vapor
      injection can hold SPF 2.5-3.0 through these zones.

  - q: "How is SPF different from HSPF2?"
    a: |
      HSPF2 is a standardized rating measured in a controlled climate profile defined by AHRI.
      SPF is the real-world measurement of how a specific heat pump performed in a specific home
      over a specific heating season. HSPF2 helps compare equipment models; SPF tells you what
      actually happened.

  - q: "Is SPF the same as SCOP?"
    a: |
      SCOP (Seasonal Coefficient of Performance) is the European equivalent of SPF, measured under
      EN 14825 with defined climate profiles for "average", "warmer", and "colder" European reference
      climates. In practice, SCOP and SPF refer to the same concept: a season-weighted average
      efficiency rather than a single test-point COP.

  - q: "Does SPF include auxiliary heat?"
    a: |
      Yes. SPF as measured in the field accounts for every kWh of electricity consumed by the system
      during the heating season, including the heat pump compressor, indoor fan, defrost cycles, and
      aux resistance heat. This is one of the reasons SPF is often lower than the rated HSPF2 or
      mild-weather COP.

  - q: "How do I calculate my heat pump's SPF?"
    a: |
      Measure (or estimate) the total heat delivered to the home during the heating season in kWh,
      and divide by the total electricity consumed by the heat pump system in the same period.
      In practice, this requires either an electricity sub-meter on the heat pump circuit and an
      estimate of heat output, or back-calculation from fuel-bill data and degree-day analysis.

  - q: "What's the difference between SPF and annual COP?"
    a: |
      "Annual COP" and "Seasonal COP" are sometimes used interchangeably with SPF, especially in
      academic literature. All refer to the same idea: dividing total useful heat output by total
      electricity input over an extended period. SPF is the most common label in residential
      contexts.

  - q: "Can SPF be higher than the rated HSPF2?"
    a: |
      Yes, in some cases. HSPF2 uses a defined climate profile that may be colder than your actual
      conditions. A heat pump installed in a milder climate, with low aux heat usage and well-sized
      to its load, can achieve a real-world SPF higher than its HSPF2 rating.

  - q: "Why is my SPF lower than expected?"
    a: |
      Common reasons for lower-than-expected SPF: aux heat running more than necessary (check
      thermostat lockout setting), an undersized system that runs aux heat frequently, ductwork
      leakage, low refrigerant charge, an installation environment colder than the rating profile,
      or short cycling.

  - q: "How does a cold-climate heat pump improve SPF?"
    a: |
      Cold-climate heat pumps use vapor-injection compressors, larger heat exchangers, and inverter
      speed control to maintain capacity at low temperatures. The result is less aux heat usage,
      higher compressor efficiency at the coldest conditions, and an SPF that holds up across the
      heating season instead of collapsing in cold weather.

  - q: "Is SPF reported by manufacturers?"
    a: |
      Manufacturers report rated efficiency metrics (HSPF2, SEER2) but not real-world SPF. SPF
      depends on the installation, climate, and operation, so it's measured in the field after
      installation. Some utility rebate programs and research projects publish SPF measurements
      across populations of installed heat pumps.

## Search intent

The search volume for "seasonal performance factor" (27,100/mo) is dominated by two reader profiles:

1. **Homeowner researching heat pumps before purchase** — has seen "HSPF2" on a brochure or comparison shopping site and is encountering "SPF" or "SCOP" in technical articles, wants to understand what these mean and which to trust
2. **Owner of an installed heat pump trying to evaluate performance** — has read about SPF in efficiency programs (e.g., NEEP, state rebate programs) and wants to know if their system is performing well

A smaller third profile: professionals (HVAC techs, building science researchers) looking for a clean reference page. They aren't the primary audience, but the article should hold up under their scrutiny.

The dominant emotional state is curiosity, not panic — this is not a "why is my system broken" query. The article should explain SPF as a clean, useful concept and connect it to decisions the reader actually makes (which heat pump to buy, whether to upgrade, how to evaluate current performance).

## Required H2 outline

Eight H2 sections. Total body target 2,200 words.

### H2 1 — What Seasonal Performance Factor Is
**Target: 250 words**

- Opens with the prose anchor (see below)
- One-sentence definition (definitional quote candidate)
- The formula explained: SPF = heat delivered / electricity in, units kWh / kWh (so dimensionless)
- Locate the concept: NOT a single test point; a measured or estimated whole-season value
- Forward-reference to H2 2 (vs COP) and H2 4 (vs HSPF2)
- Mention SCOP as the European equivalent (link to international convention)

### H2 2 — SPF vs COP
**Target: 250 words**

- COP = instantaneous efficiency at a single operating point
- SPF = season-weighted average COP, including aux heat and defrost
- Why they're not the same number: COP at 47°F is best-case, real season includes worse conditions
- A typical heat pump's rated COP at 47°F might be 3.5; its SPF in a Zone 5 home is more like 2.8
- Reference SVG: `spf-vs-cop-comparison.svg`
- Link to `/heat-pump/performance/cop-explained/` for COP detail
- End with the practical implication: don't quote COP as if it represented annual performance

### H2 3 — What Lowers SPF
**Target: 300 words**

- Outdoor temperature distribution: SPF is weighted by where the heating hours actually fall
- Reference SVG: `spf-temperature-distribution.svg`
- Aux heat consumption: every kWh of resistance heat counts in the denominator
- Defrost cycles: short periods of cooling-mode operation cost efficiency
- Part-load cycling on single-stage HPs: short cycles are less efficient
- Distribution losses: leaky ducts to unconditioned spaces waste delivered heat
- Sub-bullets covering each factor briefly

### H2 4 — SPF vs HSPF2 (and vs Older HSPF)
**Target: 300 words**

- HSPF2: AHRI-standardized rating measured under a defined climate profile (cite `ahri-210-240`)
- HSPF (old): pre-2023 version with different profile and rating points
- HSPF2 is a controlled standardized number; SPF is a real-world measurement
- Why HSPF2 still matters: it's the comparison metric across products
- Why SPF still matters: it's what you actually experience
- Reference SVG: `spf-hspf-comparison-table.svg`
- Link to `/heat-pump/performance/hspf-vs-hspf2/` for the HSPF vs HSPF2 detail
- Link to `/heat-pump/performance/seer-vs-seer2/` for the cooling-side equivalent
- Brief note on EN 14825 (SCOP) — same idea, different reference climate profiles

### H2 5 — What SPF Tells You About Your Heat Pump
**Target: 350 words**

- Typical SPF ranges by climate zone and equipment class
- Reference SVG: `spf-by-climate-chart.svg`
- The "good" SPF numbers:
  - Mild climate (zones 3-4): 3.0+ is expected
  - Mixed climate (zone 5): 2.5-3.0 typical
  - Cold climate (zones 6-7): 2.0-2.5 conventional, 2.5-3.0+ cold-climate models
  - Very cold (zone 8): cold-climate HPs only, target 2.0-2.5
- Why cold-climate models help: vapor injection, larger heat exchangers, less aux usage
- Cite NEEP field measurements
- Link to `/heat-pump/cold-climate/` and `/heat-pump/aux-heat/`
- Discuss the connection between SPF and operating cost
- End with the "compare against my electricity rate" framing

### H2 6 — Estimating Your Own SPF
**Target: 300 words**

- Two approaches: measure or estimate
- **Measure**: install an electricity sub-meter on the heat pump circuit, track over a season, calculate ratio
- **Estimate**: use fuel-bill data + degree-day analysis, back into the heat delivered, then divide by measured electricity
- The math: SPF = (heating load BTU × hours) / (electricity consumed kWh × 3,412 BTU/kWh)
- Link to `/tools/heat-pump-size-calculator/` and `/tools/seer-savings-calculator/`
- Note that some utility rebates pay extra for verified SPF performance (mention as context)
- Mention that the typical homeowner DOES NOT need to measure SPF — it's a reference concept, not an operational dashboard

### H2 7 — When SPF Is Low: Diagnostics
**Target: 250 words**

- Diagnostic order before assuming bad equipment:
  1. Thermostat aux heat lockout (most common fix)
  2. Air filter (dirty filter reduces airflow, reduces capacity, increases aux usage)
  3. Duct leakage (lost capacity)
  4. Refrigerant charge (undercharged HP can't deliver rated capacity)
  5. Outdoor unit clearance (debris reduces airflow)
- These are the same diagnostics as aux heat troubleshooting — refer to `/heat-pump/aux-heat/`
- If diagnostics pass and SPF is still below climate-zone expectation: undersized HP likely

### H2 8 — SPF in the Real World: What to Expect
**Target: 200 words**

- A close on the practical takeaway:
  - Don't expect rated efficiency in real conditions
  - Cold-climate models are worth the premium in cold zones for SPF reasons
  - Aux heat lockout settings are the single biggest user-controllable lever
  - SPF is a useful concept, not a daily reading
- Forward-reference to balance point detail, cold-climate article

## Technical depth specification

### Section 1 (Definition) — formula

```
SPF = Q_useful_heat_delivered_kWh / W_total_electricity_input_kWh
```

Where:
- Q includes useful heat delivered to conditioned space (BTU converted to kWh: 1 kWh = 3,412 BTU)
- W includes ALL electricity: compressor, indoor blower, outdoor fan, defrost, crankcase heater, aux strips

Mention this in plain language. Don't show this raw formula in the article — show the worked example instead.

### Section 6 (Estimating your own SPF) — worked example

The article should walk through a real example like this:

> A 1,800 sq ft home in climate zone 5 with a 3-ton heat pump consumes 4,200 kWh of electricity for heating over a 7-month heating season. The home's heating load was estimated at 36,000 BTU/hour at design conditions, with an average load of about 9,000 BTU/hour averaged over the heating season (factoring in the temperature distribution).
>
> Total heat delivered: 9,000 BTU/hr × 30 days/month × 24 hr/day × 7 months ÷ 3,412 BTU/kWh ≈ 13,300 kWh
> Total electricity in: 4,200 kWh
> SPF: 13,300 / 4,200 ≈ 3.2

The number can be ballparked. Don't overstate precision.

## Prose anchors

### Opening hook (use exactly)

> If a heat pump's brochure says it's rated HSPF2 9.5, what does that actually mean for your electric bill? Probably not as much as you'd hope.
>
> The Seasonal Performance Factor — SPF, or sometimes SCOP in European references — is the metric that comes closer to predicting real-world performance. It's the ratio of useful heat delivered to electricity consumed, averaged across the entire heating season. Where COP measures efficiency at a single instant and HSPF2 measures it under a standardized lab profile, SPF tells you what your specific heat pump did in your specific home over the actual winter you lived through.
>
> This article explains what SPF is, how it differs from the other efficiency metrics, what numbers to expect for different climates and equipment classes, and how to estimate it for your own system.

### Anchor paragraph — H2 2 (SPF vs COP), opening

> COP is a snapshot. SPF is a film.
>
> A heat pump's COP at 47°F might be 3.5, meaning it delivers 3.5 units of heat for every unit of electricity at that exact operating point. But during your heating season, the outdoor temperature isn't a constant 47°F. It varies from 50°F on mild fall days to single digits on the coldest nights. The heat pump's instantaneous COP varies with it — high in mild weather, low in cold weather. SPF averages all of those moments together, weighted by how many hours each occurred, and folds in defrost cycles and any aux heat that ran along the way. The result is one number that describes the whole season instead of one instant.

This paragraph is the most important rhetorical move in the article: it turns an abstract distinction (instantaneous vs averaged efficiency) into a memorable metaphor (snapshot vs film). Keep the opening sentence punchy and match the rhythm in the rest of the article.
