# FAQ

faq:
  - q: "How long does a heat pump defrost cycle last?"
    a: |
      Typical defrost cycles last 5-15 minutes. Most well-designed systems complete defrost in
      under 10 minutes. Cycles longer than 15 minutes suggest a problem — usually a stuck
      reversing valve, low refrigerant, or defrost not terminating correctly on the temperature
      sensor.

  - q: "How often should a heat pump defrost?"
    a: |
      Defrost frequency depends on outdoor temperature and humidity. In cool, humid weather
      (32-40°F at high RH), defrost may run every 30-45 minutes. In dry cold (15°F at 30% RH),
      defrost may run only every 90-120 minutes or less. Modern demand-defrost systems may
      skip defrost entirely on cold dry days. If your system defrosts every 10-20 minutes
      regardless of conditions, that suggests a defrost board or sensor problem.

  - q: "Why is my heat pump steaming outside?"
    a: |
      That's a defrost cycle in progress. The outdoor coil briefly runs hot (200°F+) to melt
      accumulated frost. The melted ice flashes to steam in the cold air, producing the
      visible plume. This is normal operation, not a sign of fire or refrigerant leak. The
      steam lasts a few minutes and disappears once defrost completes.

  - q: "Is it normal for ice to form on my heat pump?"
    a: |
      Light frost on the outdoor coil during cold humid weather is normal — that's exactly what
      the defrost cycle is designed to handle. Heavy ice that covers the coil, fan, or grille
      and persists between defrost cycles is not normal. Heavy persistent ice indicates a
      problem with defrost initiation, refrigerant charge, or airflow.

  - q: "What happens to my indoor temperature during defrost?"
    a: |
      The compressor is still running but reversed, so the indoor coil briefly cools instead
      of heats. Most systems pause the indoor blower during defrost to avoid blowing cool air,
      and may engage auxiliary electric heat to maintain indoor temperature. If you have aux
      heat, you typically won't notice defrost. Without aux, you may feel a 5-15 minute lull
      in heat output.

  - q: "Does defrost waste energy?"
    a: |
      Yes, modestly. Defrost cycles temporarily reverse heating direction, then need to ramp
      the system back up. Aux heat (if used) consumes electricity at COP=1. Real-world defrost
      energy penalty is typically 3-10% of total heating season energy, depending on climate
      and system design. AHRI HSPF2 ratings account for this in cold-climate test conditions.

  - q: "What's the difference between time defrost and demand defrost?"
    a: |
      Time-initiated defrost runs on a clock — every 30, 60, or 90 minutes — regardless of
      whether frost has actually formed. Demand-initiated defrost uses outdoor coil temperature
      and pressure sensors to detect frost buildup and only defrosts when needed. Modern systems
      (especially cold-climate certified models) use demand defrost, which saves 5-15% of
      defrost-related energy.

  - q: "My heat pump defrosts every 10 minutes — what's wrong?"
    a: |
      Defrosting every 10-15 minutes is excessive. Common causes: defrost control board
      malfunction (the board is initiating defrost too aggressively), outdoor coil temperature
      sensor reading incorrectly, low refrigerant (coil running too cold and frosting too fast),
      or restricted airflow over the outdoor coil (debris, plants too close, dirty coil).
      Schedule a service call — this is not DIY-territory and continued operation can damage
      the compressor.

  - q: "Should I clear ice off my heat pump?"
    a: |
      Light frost: no, let the defrost cycle handle it. Heavy ice covering the coil or fan:
      turn the system off first to prevent damage, then carefully remove ice with warm water.
      Never use sharp tools, hammers, or hot water above 110°F — these can damage the coil
      fins or refrigerant lines. If heavy ice forms repeatedly, the system has a defrost
      problem that needs professional diagnosis.

  - q: "Do all heat pumps have defrost cycles?"
    a: |
      All air-source heat pumps have defrost cycles. Ground-source (geothermal) heat pumps
      do not need defrost because their heat source is the ground, which stays above freezing
      year-round. Mini-split air-source heat pumps defrost, just like ducted ones. The defrost
      mechanism is universal across air-source equipment.

## Search intent

Two distinct reader cohorts:

1. **Worried homeowner** (60-70% of traffic): just noticed something unusual — steam from outdoor unit, ice on the coil, brief cool air from registers, a clunk sound, or the unit briefly going quiet. They want to know if this is normal or a problem. They are mildly to moderately alarmed.

2. **Researching heat pump buyer** (30-40%): considering or just installed a heat pump in a cold climate. They've seen forum posts about defrost cycles and want to understand the technology. They are calmly curious, not alarmed.

The article must reassure cohort 1 quickly in the opening without dismissing legitimate problems, and educate cohort 2 thoroughly in the body. Structuring as: what it is (reassurance for cohort 1), why frost forms, how the cycle works mechanically, frequency/timing, aux heat interaction, what you experience indoors, efficiency impact, cold-climate-specific behavior, and when it actually indicates a problem.

The high-volume "heat pump steaming outside" (1,300/mo) and "heat pump frozen outside" (1,600/mo) intent should be served within the first 500 words. By word 500, a worried homeowner should know "this is probably normal because X; here's how to tell if it's a problem."

This is a direct link target from brief 01 (auxiliary heat) — when readers there learn aux heat fires during defrost, this article explains why. The article should cross-link back to the aux heat hub.

### H2 1 — What a Defrost Cycle Actually Is
**Target: 250 words**

- Universal to air-source heat pumps — ducted, mini-split, packaged

- Brief preview: "We'll cover why frost forms, how the reversal works, how often it happens, what you experience, and how to spot the difference between a normal defrost and a defrost problem."

### H2 2 — Why Frost Forms on Your Heat Pump
**Target: 300 words**

- Heat pump heating mode: refrigerant evaporates inside outdoor coil, drawing heat from outdoor air
- Outdoor coil temperature drops 10-25°F below ambient air temperature
- When ambient is 32-45°F, coil temperature is at or below freezing
- Moisture in outdoor air condenses on the cold coil and freezes → frost
- Higher outdoor humidity → faster frost formation
- Lower ambient temperature → less moisture in air → slower frost formation paradoxically
- Reference SVG: frost-formation-temperature-humidity plot
- Link to /building-science/psychrometrics/dew-point/

### H2 3 — How the Defrost Cycle Works Mechanically
**Target: 400 words (the technical core)**

- The reversing valve: solenoid-controlled 4-way valve in the refrigerant circuit
- Energized one way: heating (outdoor coil = evaporator, indoor coil = condenser)
- Energized other way: defrost or cooling (outdoor coil = condenser, indoor coil = evaporator)
- Reference SVG: refrigerant-circuit-defrost showing both states
- Defrost sequence (5 stages):
  1. Defrost board signals start
  2. Indoor blower pauses (no cold air to occupants)
  3. Aux heat may engage to maintain indoor temperature
  4. Reversing valve flips (the "clunk" or "whoosh" sound)
  5. Outdoor coil rapidly warms to 100-200°F, melting frost
  6. Defrost terminates when coil reaches set temperature or after max time
- The steam: melted frost is hot enough to flash to steam when it leaves the coil into cold outdoor air
- Link to /heat-pump/aux-heat/ for aux heat detail

### H2 4 — Defrost Frequency: What's Normal
**Target: 300 words**

- Two initiation methods:
  - Time-initiated: defrost runs every 30, 60, or 90 minutes regardless of conditions (older systems)
  - Demand-initiated: defrost runs based on outdoor coil temperature and pressure (modern systems)
- Reference SVG: demand-vs-time-defrost
- Demand defrost saves 5-15% of defrost-related energy
- Frequency depends on:
  - Outdoor temperature (most defrost happens in 25-40°F range)
  - Outdoor humidity (high RH → more frequent defrost)
  - System runtime (more runtime → more frost accumulation)
  - Coil design (some cold-climate designs resist frosting better)
- A modern heat pump in 35°F humid weather may defrost every 30-45 minutes
- The same heat pump in 15°F dry weather may defrost every 90+ minutes
- A system defrosting every 10-20 minutes regardless of weather is malfunctioning

### H2 5 — Auxiliary Heat During Defrost
**Target: 250 words**

- During defrost, the indoor coil cools (it's now an evaporator)
- Without intervention, supply air would be cool and uncomfortable
- Most installations include electric resistance auxiliary heat
- Aux heat strips engage during defrost to keep supply air warm
- Aux heat runs at COP = 1.0 (every watt of electricity becomes one watt of heat)
- This is more expensive than heat pump operation (COP 2.5-4.5) but only for the 5-15 minutes of defrost
- Without aux heat (some installs), you'll feel a cool spell at the registers during defrost
- Link to /heat-pump/aux-heat/ for full aux heat treatment

### H2 6 — What You Experience Indoors
**Target: 250 words**

- Reference SVG: indoor-experience-during-defrost
- With aux heat: little to nothing — air stays warm, system runs more electricity for a few minutes
- Without aux: 5-15 minute period of cool air from registers, then back to warm
- Some thermostats display "defrost" indicator — this is correct, not an error
- Smart thermostats (Nest, Ecobee, etc.) may show "auxiliary heat active" or similar
- Sounds: a clunk or whoosh from outdoor unit (reversing valve), brief reduction in outdoor fan speed (some designs run the fan; others stop it)
- The "blowing cold air" search term lands here (link to /heat-pump/troubleshooting/blowing-cold-air/ for non-defrost causes)

### H2 7 — Defrost and Efficiency
**Target: 300 words**

- Defrost cycles consume energy without delivering heating
- Components of defrost energy cost:
  - Compressor running reversed (compressor work, partial heat recovery on the indoor side reduced to zero by blower pause)
  - Aux heat strips (if used)
  - Outdoor fan (if running)
- Real-world penalty: 3-10% of seasonal heating energy depending on climate
- Higher in cool humid climates (more frequent defrost)
- Lower in dry cold climates (less frequent defrost)
- AHRI HSPF2 incorporates defrost in rated performance under controlled test conditions
- Cold-climate certified heat pumps optimize defrost (demand-initiated, faster cycle, smarter aux timing)
- Link to /heat-pump/performance/hspf2/ and /heat-pump/performance/seasonal-performance-factor/
- Link to /tools/heat-pump-defrost-cost-calculator/

### H2 8 — Cold Climate Heat Pumps and Defrost
**Target: 300 words**

- "Cold climate" generally means designed for sustained operation below 17°F
- NEEP Cold Climate Air Source Heat Pump (CCASHP) Specification certification
- Cold-climate models use:
  - Demand-initiated defrost (not time-initiated)
  - Optimized refrigerant circuiting to resist frosting
  - Vapor injection or enhanced vapor injection (EVI) for sustained capacity at low temperatures
  - Some models use a small refrigerant accumulator and crankcase heating to handle cold restart
- Example: Mitsubishi Hyper-Heating (H2i) systems demonstrate the cold-climate approach
- Cold-climate models still defrost; they just defrost more efficiently
- Link to /heat-pump/cold-climate/cold-climate-heat-pump/
- Link to /heat-pump/cold-climate/balance-point/

### H2 9 — When Defrost Indicates a Problem
**Target: 350 words**

- Reference SVG: defrost-problem-decision-tree
- Problem indicators:
  - Frequency too high: defrost every 10-20 minutes regardless of weather
  - Duration too long: cycles running 20+ minutes (defrost not terminating)
  - Heavy ice persists between cycles
  - Entire outdoor unit becomes encased in ice
  - System runs constantly (heat pump can't keep up with load + frequent defrost)
- Common root causes:
  - Defrost control board malfunction (most common for frequency issues)
  - Outdoor coil temperature sensor (causes both frequency and duration problems)
  - Reversing valve stuck (causes duration problems and intermittent defrost failure)
  - Low refrigerant (causes frost overproduction)
  - Restricted airflow over outdoor coil (debris, dirty fins, vegetation)
- DIY checks before calling pro:
  - Outdoor unit has 24+ inches clearance on all sides
  - No debris, leaves, ice damming the bottom of the unit
  - Breaker hasn't tripped
  - Thermostat not in emergency heat mode
- Heavy ice removal: turn unit off, use warm (not hot) water, never sharp tools
- Link to /heat-pump/troubleshooting/defrost-not-working/
- Link to /heat-pump/cold-climate/sizing/ (oversizing causes excessive cycling AND defrost)
- Link to /tools/heat-pump-size-calculator/

## Technical depth specification

### Section 3 — refrigerant cycle in heating vs defrost mode

In heating mode:
- Outdoor coil (evaporator): refrigerant boils at low temperature/pressure, absorbing heat from outdoor air
- Refrigerant exits as superheated vapor at low pressure
- Compressor raises pressure, raising temperature to ~120-180°F at the indoor coil
- Indoor coil (condenser): refrigerant condenses at high temperature/pressure, releasing heat to indoor air
- Expansion device drops refrigerant pressure back to evaporator inlet conditions

In defrost mode (reversing valve flipped):
- Outdoor coil (now condenser): refrigerant condenses at high temperature/pressure, heating the outdoor coil to 100-200°F
- This rapidly melts accumulated frost
- Indoor coil (now evaporator): refrigerant boils at low temperature, absorbing heat from... indoor air (but blower is paused, so heat exchange minimized)
- Indoor coil getting cold is why blower is paused and aux heat engages

Reversing valve specifications:
- 4-port valve with sliding spool
- Solenoid-actuated, normally biased to heating position
- Switches in 1-2 seconds (the "clunk" sound)
- Failure modes: stuck in heating (no defrost), stuck mid-position (refrigerant short-circuit, severe efficiency loss), stuck in cooling/defrost (very visible — system blows cold air indoors)

### Section 4 — defrost initiation logic detail

Time-initiated defrost (older systems):
- Solid-state timer in defrost board
- Triggers defrost every N minutes if compressor has been running and outdoor temperature is below ~45°F
- Terminates defrost when outdoor coil sensor reads ~50°F OR after maximum timer expires (typically 10 minutes)
- Inefficient because it defrosts even when no frost has formed

Demand-initiated defrost (modern systems, all CCASHP):
- Outdoor coil temperature and ambient temperature both monitored
- Differential temperature (coil colder than expected vs ambient) indicates frost insulation
- Defrost initiates when differential exceeds threshold
- Terminates the same way (coil reaches set temp)
- Saves 5-15% of defrost-related energy depending on climate

### Section 7 — efficiency calculation

A typical heat pump defrost cycle in a moderately cold climate:
- Cycle duration: 8 minutes
- Compressor running reversed at ~3 kW
- Aux heat strips at ~5 kW running for 6 of 8 minutes
- Total energy during defrost: (3 kW × 8/60 hr) + (5 kW × 6/60 hr) = 0.4 + 0.5 = 0.9 kWh

If this defrost happens every 60 minutes during a 6-hour cold humid stretch:
- 6 defrost cycles × 0.9 kWh = 5.4 kWh
- Compared to no-defrost heating at COP 3.0, this is the equivalent of ~1.8 hours of full heating output spent on defrost

Over a full heating season in a place with 1,500 cold-humid hours:
- ~3-10% of seasonal heating energy goes to defrost (rough range from NREL field studies)

## Prose anchors (voice calibration)

### Opening hook (use exactly, do not rewrite)

> Your heat pump outdoor unit just produced a cloud of steam. Then the indoor blower briefly went quiet. Then you heard a thunk from outside. None of this is a problem. It is a defrost cycle, and it is the normal way an air-source heat pump handles frost on its outdoor coil.
>
> Defrost cycles happen because the outdoor coil in heating mode runs colder than the outdoor air — sometimes well below freezing. Moisture in the air condenses on the cold coil and freezes, eventually insulating the coil and reducing efficiency. The defrost cycle briefly reverses the system, makes the outdoor coil hot, and melts the frost off. Then the system goes back to heating.
>
> This article covers what's happening, why, how long it should take, what you should feel indoors during it, and how to tell a normal defrost cycle from one that indicates an actual problem.

### Anchor paragraph — H2 section 9 (When defrost indicates a problem), opening (use exactly)

> Most defrost cycles are normal. A few are not. The difference is in frequency, duration, and whether the coil clears. If the cycle runs every 45 minutes for 8 minutes and the coil is clean afterward, your heat pump is doing exactly what it was designed to do.
