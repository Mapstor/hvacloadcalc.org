# FAQ

faq:
  - q: "What size mini split do I need for a 2-car garage?"
    a: |
      For a typical 2-car garage (400-600 sq ft) with some insulation in a moderate climate,
      an 18,000 BTU (1.5 ton) mini split is a common choice. Insulated garages may get away
      with 12,000 BTU; uninsulated or in extreme climates may need 24,000 BTU. The right
      answer depends on insulation, climate, ceiling height, and how the garage is used
      (occasional vs daily occupancy).

  - q: "Is 12000 BTU enough for a 2-car garage?"
    a: |
      12,000 BTU (1 ton) is adequate for a well-insulated 2-car garage in mild climates
      (zones 1-4) used occasionally. For an uninsulated garage, daily use, hot climates, or
      cold zones below 5°F design temp, 12,000 BTU is likely undersized. Run the numbers
      based on your specific situation rather than relying on a single tonnage rule.

  - q: "Can I install a mini split in my garage myself?"
    a: |
      Pre-charged DIY mini splits (Mr. Cool, Pioneer Quick Connect, and similar) can be
      installed without HVAC technician certification because the refrigerant lines arrive
      pre-charged and don't require evacuation or charging. Standard mini splits require
      EPA Section 608 certified technicians for refrigerant work — that's federal law, not
      a recommendation. Either way, electrical work typically requires an electrician and
      a permit.

  - q: "What's the difference between a mini split and a window AC for a garage?"
    a: |
      Mini splits offer year-round heating AND cooling, run on inverter technology
      (variable-speed compressor, more efficient and quieter), have a small visible profile
      (no big window unit), and last 15-20 years with proper maintenance. Window units are
      cheaper upfront ($300-700 vs $1,500-4,000 for mini split), but provide cooling only
      (some have heat strips but inefficient), are louder, less efficient, and have shorter
      lifespan (5-10 years typical).

  - q: "Will a mini split work in an uninsulated garage?"
    a: |
      Yes, but with caveats. Uninsulated garages have higher heat loss/gain so the mini split
      will run more often and use more energy, and you'll need a larger unit (rule of thumb:
      add 50-100% to your BTU calculation). Cooling performance is more affected than heating
      because hot summer air leakage adds significant latent load. Consider insulating before
      or alongside the mini split install if budget allows — payback is usually shorter than
      most homeowners expect.

  - q: "What's the cheapest mini split that's still worth buying for a garage?"
    a: |
      Major-brand mini splits (Mitsubishi, Daikin, Fujitsu, LG, Pioneer, MrCool) start around
      $1,200-1,800 for 12,000 BTU equipment alone. Pre-charged DIY units (MrCool, Pioneer
      Quick Connect, Senville Aura, others) are at the lower end of price for self-install.
      Avoid no-name imports without AHRI certification — the warranty support and parts
      availability are typically poor and performance ratings unverified.

  - q: "Do I need a permit to install a mini split in my garage?"
    a: |
      Most US jurisdictions require permits for electrical work involving 240V circuits and
      for HVAC equipment installation. Some jurisdictions also require permits for the
      outdoor unit's location (setbacks from property lines). Check with your local building
      department before installing. Many permits are inexpensive and the inspection helps
      catch installation errors.

  - q: "Will a mini split in my detached garage work in cold weather?"
    a: |
      Cold-climate certified (NEEP CCASHP) mini splits maintain useful heating capacity down
      to 5°F or lower. Standard mini splits may struggle below 17°F. For detached garages in
      cold zones, consider either: (a) a CCASHP-rated mini split, (b) supplemental heat
      (electric resistance, propane radiant) for emergencies, or (c) accepting that on the
      coldest days, the mini split alone won't reach setpoint.

  - q: "Can I run a mini split through my attached garage wall to a workshop in the basement?"
    a: |
      No. Mini split line sets are designed for direct connection between an outdoor unit
      and one or more indoor heads in fixed locations, with maximum line set lengths typically
      50-75 feet for residential units. Routing through wall cavities and connecting to a
      different conditioned space isn't how the technology works — you'd need a separate
      indoor unit in each space (multi-zone setup) with the line sets running back to the
      outdoor unit.

  - q: "Should I get single-zone or multi-zone for garage plus another space?"
    a: |
      If you're heating/cooling just the garage, single-zone is simpler, cheaper, and more
      efficient. If you also need to condition a workshop, finished bonus room above the
      garage, or an adjacent ADU, multi-zone makes sense — one outdoor unit can serve 2-5
      indoor heads. Multi-zone outdoor units cost more upfront but save on multiple
      installations. The tradeoff is that one outdoor unit failure takes down the whole
      system.

## Technical depth specification

### Section 2 — garage load factors

For Manual J load calculation purposes, treat the garage as an unconditioned space if it's not normally heated/cooled, with the following adjustments when sizing the mini split:

**Insulation R-values (typical for garages)**:
- Walls: R-0 (uninsulated stud cavity), R-11-13 (typical insulated)
- Ceiling: R-0 to R-30 (often whatever was code at construction time)
- Floor (slab on grade): R-0 unless slab edge insulation present

**Garage door**:
- Single-layer steel: R-2 effective
- Insulated metal: R-9 to R-12
- Insulated with weather seal: R-12 to R-18
- Window panels in door reduce R substantially

**Infiltration**:
- Tight garage with good weather stripping: 5-7 ACH50
- Typical garage: 8-12 ACH50
- Loose / older garage: 15+ ACH50

**Internal gains**:
- Vehicle waste heat (10-15 min cooldown after parking warm engine)
- Lighting load
- Equipment if shop/workshop use

**Operating mode**:
- Setpoint not maintained 24/7 (different from house bedroom)
- Setback to 50°F heating / 80°F cooling typical when not in use
- Recovery time: 30-60 min from setback to comfortable
- Right-sized unit can recover quickly from setback without oversizing

### Section 3 — sizing rule-of-thumb math

For a quick planning estimate (NOT a Manual J substitute):

```
BTU_cooling = (sq_ft × 30-40 BTU/sq ft) × insulation_factor × climate_factor

insulation_factor:
  Uninsulated: 1.5-2.0
  Partial insulation: 1.2-1.5
  Full insulation: 1.0

climate_factor:
  Zones 1-2 (hot): 1.3 for cooling
  Zones 3-4 (mid): 1.0
  Zones 5-8 (cold): 0.7 for cooling, but check heating separately

BTU_heating = BTU_cooling × climate_heating_factor
  Zones 1-2: 0.3
  Zones 3-4: 0.8
  Zone 5: 1.1
  Zone 6-7: 1.4
  Zone 8: 1.8
```

Pick the larger of BTU_cooling and BTU_heating for the equipment size.

Example: 500 sq ft 2-car garage in zone 5, partial insulation:
- BTU_cooling = 500 × 35 × 1.3 × 0.7 = 15,925 (round to 18,000 BTU)
- BTU_heating = 18,000 × 1.1 = 19,800 (round to 24,000 BTU for safety margin)
- Choose 24,000 BTU unit with CCASHP rating

### Section 7 — CCASHP performance specifics

NEEP CCASHP certification requires:
- Maintains ≥75% of rated capacity at 5°F outdoor temperature
- HSPF2 ≥ 8.5 (varies by tier)
- COP ≥ 1.75 at 5°F

Standard mini splits typically deliver only 50-60% of rated capacity at 5°F and may not run reliably below 0°F.

NEEP lists 100+ certified products from major manufacturers. Verify your specific model number on the list before purchasing for a cold-climate garage.

### Section 9 — DIY install legal/safety boundary

EPA Section 608 (Clean Air Act) regulates refrigerant handling. Civil penalty for unauthorized handling is significant.

What's allowed without certification:
- Pre-charged DIY mini-splits where lines come pre-charged and connect via quick-connect fittings
- Replacing or accessing parts that don't involve opening the refrigerant circuit

What requires Section 608 certification:
- Installing standard (not pre-charged) mini splits
- Adding refrigerant to existing system
- Recovering refrigerant
- Brazing line sets

For DIY pre-charged install, the legal boundary holds because lines aren't broken. The skills required: drilling/sealing wall penetration (moderate), running electrical conduit (moderate, electrician may be needed for code), low-voltage control wiring (basic).
