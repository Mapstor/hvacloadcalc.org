faq:
  - q: "How many BTU do I need for 1,000 square feet?"
    a: |
      For a typical 1,000 sq ft space with standard 8-foot ceilings, moderate climate, and
      normal occupancy, plan for around 18,000-21,000 BTU (1.5-1.75 tons of cooling). Adjust
      up for hot climates (zone 1-2: add 30%), high ceilings, sun-exposed spaces, or
      poor insulation. Adjust down for cool climates (zones 5-8) or well-insulated spaces.

  - q: "How many BTU per square foot do I need for an AC?"
    a: |
      Rule of thumb for cooling: 20-30 BTU per square foot in moderate climates. Hot/humid
      climates (zones 1-2) push higher (30-35 BTU/sq ft). Cooler climates (zones 5-7) can
      use less (15-20 BTU/sq ft). For windowed rooms with sun exposure or higher ceilings,
      adjust upward. These rules are starting points, not final answers — a Manual J load
      calculation gives a more precise answer for whole-house central AC.

  - q: "Is 5,000 BTU enough for a bedroom?"
    a: |
      Yes, for a typical bedroom of 100-150 sq ft with one or two occupants, standard
      ceiling, and moderate climate. For larger bedrooms (200+ sq ft), go up to 6,000-8,000
      BTU. For master bedrooms with attached bathrooms or walk-in closets, treat as 200-300
      sq ft total. Window unit 5,000 BTU is the most common entry-level size; 8,000 BTU is
      the common second tier.

  - q: "How many tons of AC for 2,000 sq ft?"
    a: |
      For a typical 2,000 sq ft single-family home with standard insulation in a moderate
      climate, plan for 3 tons (36,000 BTU) of cooling capacity. Adjust up for hot climates,
      poor insulation, or many windows; adjust down for excellent insulation, cool climates,
      or modest occupancy. Get a Manual J for the final answer before installing equipment.

  - q: "What's the difference between BTU and tonnage?"
    a: |
      Tonnage is a way of expressing BTU/hr capacity for air conditioners. 1 ton of AC =
      12,000 BTU/hr of cooling capacity. So a 2-ton AC is 24,000 BTU/hr; a 3-ton AC is
      36,000 BTU/hr; a 5-ton AC is 60,000 BTU/hr. Window and portable units are usually
      rated in BTU; central AC and heat pumps are usually rated in tons. The underlying
      capacity is the same — different conventions for different equipment classes.

  - q: "What happens if I get an AC that's too big?"
    a: |
      Oversized ACs short-cycle: they cool the air temperature down quickly but don't run
      long enough to remove humidity. The result is a cold, clammy feeling indoor environment
      and higher energy use from startup losses. Compressor life is also reduced because of
      the frequent starts. Variable-speed (inverter) ACs tolerate moderate oversizing better
      than single-stage units.

  - q: "Can I use a portable AC's BTU rating the same as a window AC's?"
    a: |
      Not directly. Portable ACs are rated using outdated DOE standards that often overstate
      real-world performance, especially for single-hose designs (which exhaust hot air using
      conditioned indoor air, reducing effective cooling). Real-world cooling output of a
      single-hose portable AC is typically 20-30% less than its nameplate BTU rating. Plan
      to size up by one tier when choosing portable over window AC.

  - q: "How do high ceilings affect BTU needs?"
    a: |
      Higher ceilings = more volume to cool. Standard 8-foot ceiling is the baseline.
      9-foot ceilings need ~10% more BTU. 10-foot ceilings need ~20% more. Cathedral
      ceilings (12+ ft) need 25-40% more. The relationship isn't perfectly linear because
      heat rises and the upper volume is less occupant-affected.

  - q: "Does insulation affect the right BTU?"
    a: |
      Yes, substantially. Poor insulation (below current code) increases cooling load by
      20-40%. Excellent insulation (above current code, modern construction) can reduce
      cooling load by 10-15%. Both insulation and air sealing matter — leaky homes need
      more cooling capacity to handle infiltration loads.

  - q: "Is this chart good enough for buying central AC?"
    a: |
      For initial planning and comparison, yes. For actually buying central AC, no — get a
      Manual J load calculation from your installer or hire one independently. Central AC
      installations are expensive and a Manual J is much more accurate than any chart.
      Window units and portable ACs are cheaper, so the cost of being wrong is lower; charts
      are usually fine for those.

## Technical depth specification

### Section 1 — chart values and rationale

The hero chart values come from ENERGY STAR's room AC sizing guide, calibrated to:
- ~20-25 BTU per sq ft baseline for moderate climates
- 8-foot ceilings
- Normal occupancy
- No kitchen or sun room
- Average insulation

These align with Manual J output for the same scenarios within ±10%. The chart is a "starting point" that gets adjusted by section 3's factors.

### Section 3 — adjustment math

Combined adjustment formula:
```
adjusted_BTU = base_BTU × ceiling_factor × climate_factor × space_type_factor × insulation_factor × (1 + occupancy_adjustment) × (1 + kitchen_adjustment)
```

Where:
- ceiling_factor: 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4 for 8, 9, 10, 11, 12+ ft
- climate_factor: 1.30, 1.15, 1.05, 1.00, 0.90, 0.85, 0.80 for zones 1-7
- space_type_factor: 1.0-2.0 depending on space
- insulation_factor: 1.30 (poor), 1.0 (average), 0.85-0.90 (good)
- occupancy_adjustment: 0 baseline (1-2 people), +0.05 per extra person above 2
- kitchen_adjustment: 0 baseline, +0.2 if kitchen is in cooled space

### Section 4 — climate zone underpinning

IECC climate zones derive from ASHRAE Standard 169 climatic data:
- Zone 1: tropical (Miami, Honolulu)
- Zone 2: hot/humid southern US
- Zone 3: mixed/warm southern US
- Zone 4: mixed mid-US (Ohio Valley, mid-Atlantic)
- Zone 5: cool northern US
- Zone 6: cold northern US
- Zone 7-8: very cold (interior Alaska)

Cooling adjustment factor approximates:
- Latent (humidity) load impact
- Higher design temperatures
- Solar load intensity
- Diurnal range (cool zones have wider day-night swings, often reducing cooling needs)

### Section 5 — worked example math

**Example B (1,500 sq ft single-story home, zone 2):**

Step 1: Chart baseline for 1,500 sq ft = ~27,000-30,000 BTU (note: chart row for 1,400-1,800 sq ft shows 30,000 BTU)

Step 2: Climate adjustment, zone 2 = ×1.15-1.20

Step 3: Ceiling height adjustment, 10 ft = ×1.20

Combined: 30,000 × 1.18 (zone 2 mid) × 1.20 = 42,480 BTU

Round to standard equipment size: 42,000 BTU = 3.5 tons OR 48,000 BTU = 4 tons.

A 3.5-ton system is probably right; verify with Manual J.

### Section 8 — equipment performance

**Window AC**:
- Tested per DOE Window AC standards
- Single-hose design simplest, dual-hose more efficient
- 5,000 BTU window units typically draw 4-6 amps (450-700 W)

**Portable AC**:
- Single-hose: ~20-30% efficiency penalty vs window units
- Dual-hose: ~10-15% efficiency penalty vs window units
- DOE recently updated test standards; older listings overstate performance

**Mini split**:
- AHRI 210/240 rated, accurate to ±5%
- Variable-speed compressor modulates output (modulation range 30-110% of rated typical)
- SEER2 ratings 17-30+ standard

**Central AC**:
- AHRI 210/240 rated
- Output depends on indoor temperature and humidity (real-world deviates from rated)
- Manual J + Manual S sizes correctly
