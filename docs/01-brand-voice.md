# 01 — Brand Voice

## The voice in one sentence

Write like an experienced HVAC technician explaining things over coffee to a homeowner who's smart, skeptical, and ready to learn — never like a marketing copywriter, a textbook, or a generic AI.

## Voice attributes (use these as a checklist)

| Attribute | Yes | No |
|---|---|---|
| Tone | Direct, technical, calm | Promotional, hedgy, hype |
| Authority | Earned through specifics | Claimed through adjectives |
| Pace | Short sentences, varied rhythm | Long flowing prose blocks |
| Stance | Honest about uncertainty | Pretends to certainty it doesn't have |
| Reader frame | "Here's how it works" | "We're here to help you" |
| Numbers | Specific, sourced | Vague ranges, unsourced |
| Edge cases | Acknowledged, explained | Glossed over |
| Decisions | Helps reader decide | Decides for the reader |

## Sentence structure rules

- **Average sentence length: 14-18 words.** Mix short (5-10 word) and medium (20-25 word) sentences. Avoid the AI-generated "all sentences are 22 words" cadence.
- **One idea per sentence.** Split compound sentences when they get awkward.
- **Start sentences with varied words.** Never start three consecutive sentences with the same word. Never start three consecutive sentences with "The."
- **Active voice by default.** Passive voice only when the actor genuinely doesn't matter.

## Paragraph rules (also in `04-content-policy.md`, repeating here because they're voice-critical)

- **Max 4 sentences per paragraph.** Sweet spot is 2-3.
- **No "wall of text" paragraphs.** If a paragraph hits 4 sentences, ask whether it should be a list, a callout, or split.
- **Vary paragraph length.** Mix one-sentence paragraphs (for emphasis) with normal ones. Never have five 3-sentence paragraphs in a row.

## Forbidden phrases (do not use, ever)

These are AI-tell phrases and marketing clichés. Every one of them gets caught by editorial review:

- "In today's world"
- "It's important to note"
- "It's worth noting"
- "When it comes to"
- "Let's dive in" / "let's dive into"
- "Navigate the world of"
- "Look no further"
- "Whether you're [a homeowner] or [a contractor]"
- "—after all,"
- "At the end of the day"
- "In essence"
- "Plays a [crucial/vital/key] role"
- "A [crucial/vital/key] aspect"
- "It goes without saying"
- "The bottom line"
- "Pro tip:" / "Quick tip:"
- "Did you know"
- "Believe it or not"
- "Game changer"
- "Cutting-edge"
- "State-of-the-art"
- "Robust solution"
- "Seamlessly"
- "Holistic"

## Forbidden patterns (structural anti-patterns)

- **Parallel construction loops**: "X is Y. But Y is also Z. And Z connects to A." Robotic. Vary.
- **Listicle inflation**: don't pad a 4-item list to 10 by adding weak items. Better to have 4 strong items.
- **The "in this article" intro**: never tell the reader what the article will cover. The headings do that.
- **The "in conclusion" outro**: never summarize at the end. The article ends when the last useful thing has been said.
- **Question headings used as filler**: H2s should be statements or specific questions a reader actually has. "What is X?" is fine when X needs definition. "Let's understand X" is not.
- **"As we mentioned earlier"**: never. Either repeat the idea cleanly or trust the reader to remember.

## Words to use sparingly

These are not forbidden but cap usage:

- "Essentially" — max 1 per article
- "Generally" — max 2 per article (and prefer specifics)
- "Typically" — max 3 per article (and prefer specifics with sources)
- "Often" — max 3 per article
- "Significant" / "significantly" — max 2 per article
- "Various" — max 1 per article
- "Crucial" / "vital" / "essential" / "important" — combined max 2 per article

When you find yourself reaching for one of these, ask: can I give a specific number, range, or condition instead?

## How to introduce a concept

**Bad** (generic, AI-flavored):
> When it comes to sizing your heat pump, there are many important factors to consider. It's worth noting that proper sizing is essential for efficiency and comfort.

**Good** (specific, sourced, conversational):
> Heat pump sizing comes down to one number: BTU/hour at your local design temperature. Get that right and the system runs efficiently for 15+ years. Get it wrong by 25% and you'll either freeze on the coldest day or short-cycle through every shoulder season.

The difference: specifics, stakes, and a slight edge of opinion grounded in physics.

## How to explain a number

Always show:
1. **What the number is** (concrete value)
2. **Where it comes from** (source or formula)
3. **Why it matters** (decision implication)
4. **Range of normal** (so reader can sanity-check their own situation)

**Bad**:
> A typical home needs about 30 BTU per square foot.

**Good**:
> Most US homes need 25–35 BTU per square foot for cooling, depending on climate and insulation. A 2,000 sq ft home in Atlanta (climate zone 3, average 1990s construction) typically calculates to 50,000–60,000 BTU/hour total cooling load. Your number can be higher in Phoenix or lower in Seattle. [ACCA Manual J 8th Ed. design loads]

## How to handle uncertainty

Be honest. The phrase "depends on" is fine if followed by what it depends on. The phrase "every situation is different" is filler — replace with the specific dimensions that vary.

**Bad**:
> Heat pump efficiency varies by climate.

**Good**:
> Heat pump efficiency drops as outdoor temperature falls. A unit rated 10.0 HSPF in lab conditions delivers closer to 8.5 HSPF in climate zone 5 and 7.0 HSPF in climate zone 6, because most heating hours happen below the 47°F rating temperature.

## How to handle disputed topics

Where building scientists disagree (e.g., whether to oversize a heat pump for cold-climate retrofit), present both positions, cite both sides, then state the reader's decision criteria. Do not pretend a consensus exists where it doesn't.

## How to handle the homeowner who's stressed

A lot of traffic will come from homeowners who just got a scary HVAC quote or noticed something alarming. Open with acknowledgment of the situation, not with definitions.

**Bad opener** (BTU calculator page):
> A BTU is a British Thermal Unit, which is a measurement of heat energy.

**Good opener**:
> If your contractor recommended a 5-ton AC for a 1,500 sq ft house, this calculator will tell you what size you actually need. The math behind it is below.

Lead with utility. Definitions come after they're earned.

## How to handle "should I" questions

Articles will get questions like "Should I oversize my heat pump?" Don't dodge. State the considered answer, then the conditions under which it changes.

**Bad**:
> Whether to oversize depends on many factors. Consult an HVAC professional.

**Good**:
> No, with two exceptions. The default rule is to size for the calculated heating load at your 99% design temperature, not above. Oversizing causes short-cycling, poor humidity control, and reduced equipment life. The two exceptions are: (1) cold-climate retrofits where your existing fossil-fuel system masked actual heat loss; (2) homes planning major envelope upgrades within 5 years.

## Voice in calculator pages

Calculator pages have less prose. The voice rules still apply, but with these adjustments:

- Lead with the calculator visible above the fold
- One paragraph of context (3 sentences max) before the tool
- Below the tool: methodology, worked example, edge cases, FAQ, sources
- Use H2/H3 hierarchy to make scannable
- The worked example uses the same default values the calculator opens with

## Voice in glossary/definition pages

These should be quotable by LLMs. Optimize for clean extractability:

- First sentence: a complete one-sentence definition that stands alone
- Second sentence: the most important practical implication
- Then: full explanation, examples, related terms
- Always include the formula or numerical relationship if there is one

Example:

> **CFM (cubic feet per minute)** is the standard measure of airflow in HVAC systems, equal to the volume of air a fan or duct moves in 60 seconds.
>
> Residential HVAC systems are sized at approximately 400 CFM per ton of cooling capacity, which is why a 3-ton (36,000 BTU) air handler is typically rated for around 1,200 CFM.

The first two sentences are quotable in isolation. That's the goal.

## Final voice gut-check

Before any article ships, ask:

1. Would an experienced HVAC tech reading this think "yeah, that's right"?
2. Would a homeowner reading this learn something they could use tomorrow?
3. Could a Reddit user paste a paragraph as a useful answer to someone's question?
4. Does any sentence feel like it could be from a generic blog post on any topic?
5. Did the article tell me something specific that I couldn't get from a 30-second skim of competitor pages?

If 1-4 are yes and 5 is yes, the voice is right.
