faq:
  - q: "How short is too short for an AC cycle?"
    a: |
      Normal AC cycles run 10-15 minutes once the home is up to temperature, with longer
      cycles during hot weather. Cycles shorter than 5-7 minutes are short cycling. Cycles
      of 1-2 minutes indicate severe short cycling and almost always have a fixable cause.
      Note that variable-speed inverter ACs do not "cycle" in the same sense — they modulate
      output, so short cycling does not apply.

  - q: "Will short cycling damage my AC?"
    a: |
      Yes, over time. The compressor draws several times its running current during startup.
      Frequent startups stress the compressor windings, the contactor, and the start capacitor.
      A short-cycling AC typically reaches end-of-life in 6-8 years rather than the 12-15 years
      a normally-cycling unit lasts.

  - q: "Is short cycling dangerous?"
    a: |
      Not dangerous to people, but bad for the equipment. The risks are compressor failure,
      tripped breakers from repeated startup current, and ice buildup on the evaporator coil
      which can damage the refrigerant lines if it forms a complete blockage. None of these
      pose a safety risk to occupants.

  - q: "Can a dirty filter really cause short cycling?"
    a: |
      Yes. A dirty filter restricts airflow over the evaporator coil. With less air moving
      across the coil, the coil temperature drops below freezing and ice forms. The ice further
      restricts airflow, the coil temperature drops further, the system either trips on low
      pressure or freezes solid and shuts off. Change the filter, let the ice melt completely,
      and run the system. If the problem returns, the filter wasn't the only cause.

  - q: "Why does my AC short cycle only when it's hot outside?"
    a: |
      Three likely reasons: refrigerant level is borderline (only short cycles under high heat
      load), the outdoor condenser coil is dirty (can't reject heat efficiently when the air
      is already hot), or the system is undersized for extreme conditions (rare; oversized is
      far more common).

  - q: "Can I fix AC short cycling myself?"
    a: |
      Some causes, yes. Filter changes, condenser coil cleaning, thermostat battery replacement,
      and outdoor unit clearance are all homeowner-friendly. Refrigerant work specifically
      requires EPA Section 608 certification — handling refrigerants without it is a federal
      violation. Frozen evaporator coil, suspected refrigerant issues, and electrical component
      replacement are all professional service calls.

  - q: "How much does it cost to fix an AC that's short cycling?"
    a: |
      DIY fixes (filter, cleaning) cost $5-50. Thermostat replacement runs $150-400 installed.
      Refrigerant recharge with leak repair typically runs $300-1,500 depending on leak location
      and refrigerant type. Capacitor replacement runs $150-300. Compressor replacement runs
      $1,500-3,000 — usually the trigger to consider replacing the whole unit instead.

  - q: "My AC is short cycling but it's a brand new system — what now?"
    a: |
      Most common causes for new systems: oversized for the home (Manual J wasn't done correctly),
      thermostat placement (in direct sun, near a vent, or on an exterior wall), wiring error
      in installation, or a defective control board. Contact the installer first — this is
      almost certainly under warranty.

  - q: "What's the difference between AC short cycling and the compressor failing?"
    a: |
      A short-cycling AC starts up and runs briefly, then shuts off, then restarts. A failing
      compressor either won't start at all, hums without starting (capacitor or compressor
      failure), trips the breaker, or makes loud mechanical noises. Short cycling can lead to
      compressor failure if left unaddressed long enough.

  - q: "Does short cycling waste electricity?"
    a: |
      Significantly. Startup draws several times the running current and contributes nothing
      to cooling — the system reaches steady-state efficiency 1-3 minutes into a cycle. A
      short-cycling AC running 2-minute cycles spends a large fraction of its energy in startup
      mode, achieving little cooling. Real-world energy waste is typically 30-60% above what
      a properly cycling unit would use to deliver the same cooling.

## Search intent

This is a high-stress search. Most people typing "AC short cycling" are sitting in a house that isn't cooling properly, with the unit clicking on and off audibly every few minutes. They are alarmed, want to know if it's expensive, and want to know if they can do anything about it themselves before calling a contractor (who will charge $150+ for a service visit).

The high CPC ($21+) reflects this commercial intent. AdSense ads will be expensive on this page, which is the point — we are not directing readers to a contractor referral service, we are providing the information they need to act on the problem themselves where possible, and helping them know what to ask when they do call a professional.

Three sub-intents to serve:
1. **What is it?** (`what is short cycling`, 720/mo) — a small share of readers don't know the term yet
2. **Why is mine doing this?** (`ac short cycling causes`, 390/mo + variants ~3,000/mo combined) — the dominant intent
3. **How do I fix it?** (`how to fix short cycling`, 320/mo + cost variants) — the conversion intent

The article structures intent in this order: brief definition (~150 words), then 7 ranked causes with diagnosis for each, then what short cycling costs, then DIY vs Pro decisions, then cost of fixes. The reader can stop at any point and have actionable information.

The article also serves furnace short cycling traffic (1,900/mo) via a cross-link in the conclusion, even though the article itself is AC-focused. The two problems are mechanically distinct but the diagnostic framework is similar.

### H2 2 — Cause 1: Oversized AC
**Target: 350 words (the highest-impact cause to explain)**

- Most common structural cause and the only one that can't be "fixed" without replacing the equipment
- Mechanism: oversized AC cools the air to setpoint in 2-3 minutes, then shuts off; the compressor hasn't run long enough to remove humidity or hit steady-state efficiency
- Reference SVG: oversized-cycling-comparison
- Why oversizing is common (link to /manual-j/ and /manual-s/)
- Manual S allows oversizing up to 15-25% above Manual J cooling load; beyond that, short cycling
- Diagnosis: this cause is suspected when the AC is reasonably new, well-maintained, but cycles rapidly even on hot days
- The fix is replacement with a correctly sized unit; downsize on next replacement or add a variable-capacity unit
- Link to /ac/oversizing/ for detailed treatment
- Link to /tools/ac-sizing-calculator/ and /tools/manual-j-calculator/

### H2 3 — Cause 2: Dirty Air Filter
**Target: 300 words**

- The most common cause and almost always DIY-fixable
- Mechanism: restricted airflow → evaporator coil temperature drops below freezing → ice forms → airflow restricts further → system trips or shuts off → repeat
- Reference EPA/ENERGY STAR filter change recommendations (1-3 months depending on type)
- Diagnosis: pull the filter; if it's gray and matted, it's the problem
- The fix: replace the filter and let any ice on the coil melt completely (turn AC off for 2-4 hours)
- If short cycling returns after a filter change with no ice: filter wasn't the only issue
- Link to /ac/maintenance/filter-change-frequency/

### H2 4 — Cause 3: Frozen Evaporator Coil
**Target: 250 words**

- Often downstream of cause 2 (dirty filter) or cause 4 (low refrigerant)
- Mechanism: same as cause 2 — coil drops below freezing, ice forms, airflow blocks
- Diagnosis: ice visible on the indoor coil or on the refrigerant lines coming out of the air handler
- The fix: turn the system off, let the ice melt completely (don't chip it off), then run the system
- If ice returns within 24 hours after filter is clean: refrigerant or airflow issue, call a pro
- Link to /ac/troubleshooting/frozen-evaporator-coil/

### H2 5 — Cause 4: Low Refrigerant (Leak)
**Target: 300 words**

- One of the most common pro-only causes
- Mechanism: lower refrigerant pressure → lower evaporator temperature → freezing → cycling
- Reference SVG: refrigerant-circuit-low-charge
- Note that refrigerant doesn't "get used up" — if there's less, there's a leak somewhere
- Topping off without finding the leak is a temporary fix at best
- EPA Section 608 certification REQUIRED by federal law for any refrigerant work
- Diagnosis: typically by gauges + leak detection (electronic detector, UV dye, soap solution)
- Cost range: $300-1,500 for leak detection + repair + recharge depending on leak location and refrigerant type
- Note: R-22 systems are increasingly expensive to recharge as R-22 phases out; major leak in R-22 system often triggers replacement decision
- Link to /ac/troubleshooting/low-refrigerant/

### H2 6 — Cause 5: Dirty Condenser Coil
**Target: 250 words**

- The outdoor coil — easy to clean, often neglected
- Mechanism: dirty fins can't reject heat → high refrigerant pressure → safety switch trips → cycle restarts → repeat
- Diagnosis: pull the outdoor unit cover and look at the fins; if matted with dust, leaves, cottonwood seeds, or grass clippings, it needs cleaning
- DIY-friendly: garden hose from inside-out at low pressure, fin comb for bent fins
- Avoid pressure washers (damages fins)
- Annual cleaning recommended per ENERGY STAR maintenance checklist
- Link to /ac/troubleshooting/dirty-condenser-coil/

### H2 7 — Cause 6: Thermostat Issues
**Target: 250 words**

- Less common but a real cause in some installs
- Three sub-causes:
  - Bad placement: thermostat in direct sun, near a supply register, on an exterior wall (reads wrong temperature)
  - Dead/low batteries: erratic readings, intermittent cycling
  - Wiring or programming: short cycling differential set too tight (e.g., 0.5°F instead of 1-2°F)
- Diagnosis: visually check placement, swap batteries, check anticipator or cycle-rate settings
- DIY-friendly: batteries and basic settings
- Smart thermostat-specific issues: see brand-specific articles (link to /heat-pump/aux-heat/ecobee/ for Ecobee context — same logic applies to AC stages)
- Link to /ac/troubleshooting/thermostat-placement/

### H2 8 — Cause 7: Electrical Issues
**Target: 250 words**

- Capacitor, contactor, control board, low voltage from utility — all can cause short cycling
- Capacitor: most common — fails over time, especially in hot climates; symptoms include hum without start, intermittent starting, short runtime
- Contactor: relay that switches the compressor circuit; pitted contacts cause intermittent contact
- Control board: most expensive; often misdiagnosed (other causes look like control board failure)
- Diagnosis: pro service call with multimeter
- Cost ranges: capacitor $150-300, contactor $200-400, control board $400-900
- Strongly discourage DIY on electrical components (high voltage, capacitors store charge)

### H2 9 — What Short Cycling Costs You
**Target: 300 words**

- Energy: 30-60% waste vs properly cycling
- Comfort: humidity rises (AC isn't running long enough to remove latent load), temperature swings
- Reference SVG: cost-of-short-cycling four-metric comparison
- Lifespan: 6-8 years instead of 12-15
- Specific dollar example: a 2-ton AC running 1,500 hours/year at 1.6 kW (short-cycling) vs 1.0 kW (normal) at $0.16/kWh = $384 vs $240, a $144/year difference

- Pro required: refrigerant (federal law), electrical components, repeated short cycling after DIY fixes

## Technical depth specification

### Section 1 — define cycling rigorously

A "cycle" in AC operation is one full ON → OFF → ON sequence of the compressor. Cycle length is the ON time within one cycle. Cycle frequency is cycles per hour.

AHRI 210/240 rates equipment under steady-state operation; minimum cycle behavior in field operation comes from thermostat differential settings and house thermal mass, not equipment design directly. A typical residential thermostat has a 1-2°F differential, which combined with typical home thermal capacitance produces 3-4 cycles per hour at moderate cooling load. Short cycling is operationally defined as 6+ cycles per hour or under-5-minute ON periods.

### Section 2 — oversizing physics

Cooling has two components:
- **Sensible cooling**: changing the air temperature
- **Latent cooling**: removing moisture (condensing it on the coil)

A correctly sized AC runs long enough per cycle that the evaporator coil reaches steady-state operation (1-3 minutes) and then runs another 7-12 minutes condensing moisture. An oversized AC reaches the thermostat setpoint in 2-3 minutes — before steady-state cooling efficiency is reached and before significant latent cooling has occurred. The home is at temperature but humid.

Manual S oversizing tolerances:
- Single-stage AC: not more than 15% over Manual J cooling load
- Two-stage AC: not more than 20% over Manual J cooling load
- Variable-speed: not more than 25% over Manual J cooling load

### Section 5 — refrigerant cycle and pressure

The refrigerant cycle works on pressure-temperature relationships. R-410A (current standard) operates at approximately 70-80 psi suction, 250-350 psi liquid in typical conditions. If charge is low:
- Suction pressure drops
- Evaporator temperature drops (the pressure-temperature relationship)
- Evaporator goes below 32°F → freezes
- Compressor runs in vapor envelope, may trip on low pressure switch
- System shuts down briefly, restarts, repeats

R-22 (legacy refrigerant, phased out for new equipment) operates at different pressures (~70 psi suction, 200-250 psi liquid). R-32 and R-454B are emerging next-gen refrigerants.

Note: per EPA Section 608, only certified technicians may handle refrigerant. Civil penalties apply.

### Section 8 — cost math (use exact electricity rate and assumptions)

```
Cost example: 2-ton AC, 1,500 cooling hours per year

Normal operation:
  Power draw at steady state: ~1.0 kW
  Energy per year: 1.0 kW × 1,500 hr = 1,500 kWh
  Cost: 1,500 × $0.16 = $240/year

Short-cycling operation:
  Power draw averaged over startup-heavy cycles: ~1.6 kW
  Energy per year: 1.6 kW × 1,500 hr = 2,400 kWh
  Cost: 2,400 × $0.16 = $384/year

Difference: $144/year
```
