# FAQ
# ============================================================
faq:
  - q: "What is a good U-factor for windows?"
    a: |
      In most US climates, U-0.30 or lower is considered good for residential windows. The
      2021 IECC code maximum is U-0.30 in zones 5-8 and U-0.32 in zones 3-4. ENERGY STAR
      Most Efficient certification ranges from U-0.26 in warm climates to U-0.20 in cold ones.
      A window with U-0.25 will outperform a U-0.32 window noticeably; a window below U-0.20
      is at the leading edge.

  - q: "What's the difference between U-factor and R-value?"
    a: |
      They measure the same thing in opposite directions. R-value measures resistance to heat
      flow (higher is better); U-factor measures conductance (lower is better). U = 1/R. The
      industry uses R-value for insulation (walls, attic, basement) and U-factor for windows
      and doors. A U-0.30 window has an R-value of 3.3, which is low compared to most wall
      sections (R-13 to R-21).

  - q: "What does an NFRC label show?"
    a: |
      The NFRC (National Fenestration Rating Council) label shows four main values: U-factor
      (heat flow rate, lower is better), Solar Heat Gain Coefficient or SHGC (fraction of
      solar heat passing through, lower means less summer heat gain), Visible Transmittance
      or VT (how much visible light passes through, higher is brighter), and Air Leakage
      (how much air leaks through the window assembly, lower is better). All certified
      windows sold in the US must show NFRC ratings.

  - q: "Are triple-pane windows worth it?"
    a: |
      It depends on climate and budget. In cold climates (zones 5-8), triple-pane windows
      cut U-factor by 30-50% compared to double-pane Low-E, which improves comfort and
      reduces heating cost. The upgrade cost from double-pane Low-E to triple-pane is
      typically $3-8 per square foot. Payback periods range from 10-25 years depending on
      climate, fuel cost, and window area. In warmer climates, the comfort benefit is smaller
      and payback is much longer.

  - q: "What is Low-E coating and how does it affect U-factor?"
    a: |
      Low-E (low-emissivity) coatings are microscopic metallic layers on one or more glass
      surfaces. They reflect long-wave infrared radiation (heat) while letting visible light
      pass through. Adding a single Low-E coating to a double-pane window typically lowers
      U-factor from around U-0.50 to U-0.35. Adding two Low-E coatings (one per inner
      surface) further lowers U-factor toward U-0.30 or below.

  - q: "Does argon gas really make a difference in U-factor?"
    a: |
      Yes, modestly. Argon is denser than air, so it slows convective heat transfer between
      panes. A double-pane window with argon fill typically has 5-10% lower U-factor than
      the same window with air fill. Krypton (rarer, more expensive) further reduces U-factor,
      mostly used in narrow gap triple-pane windows. The gas fill effect is smaller than
      Low-E coating effect but noticeable.

  - q: "What U-factor is required by code?"
    a: |
      Under 2021 IECC Section R402: Zone 1 maximum U-1.20, Zone 2 U-0.40, Zone 3 U-0.32,
      Zone 4 (except marine) U-0.32, Zones 5-8 U-0.30. These are maximum allowable values
      for new construction; existing windows are typically grandfathered. Many states have
      adopted IECC 2021; others use earlier versions or state-specific amendments. Check
      with your local building department.

  - q: "Why does my U-0.27 window still feel cold near the glass?"
    a: |
      Even a high-performance window has dramatically lower insulation value than a wall.
      A U-0.27 window has R-value 3.7; a typical R-19 wall has 5× the insulation value.
      Cold air falls against window glass even in good windows, creating a "drafty" feeling
      that isn't actually a draft. Window treatments (curtains, cellular shades) help by
      adding thermal resistance, especially at night. If you feel actual drafts, the issue
      may be air leakage around the frame, not the window's U-factor.

  - q: "Does U-factor change with temperature?"
    a: |
      Slightly. NFRC measures U-factor at standard winter conditions (-18°F outdoor, 70°F
      indoor, 12.3 mph wind). Actual U-factor varies a few percent with temperature
      difference and wind speed, but for practical comparisons, the NFRC value is what
      matters. The label value is the comparison standard between products.

  - q: "Is replacing windows for U-factor improvements cost-effective?"
    a: |
      Window replacement for energy savings alone usually has a payback of 15-30 years —
      long compared to other envelope upgrades. Window replacement is usually most cost-
      effective when (1) the existing windows have failed seals or rot (replacement was
      needed anyway), (2) the home is in a very cold climate with high heating bills, or
      (3) bundled with aesthetic upgrades. For pure energy savings, prioritize air sealing,
      attic insulation, and wall insulation before windows.

## Search intent

Two main cohorts:

1. **Window shoppers** (~70%): considering or in the middle of window purchase, trying to make sense of NFRC labels at the showroom or online retailer. They want to know what U-factor means, what a "good" number is, how to compare products.

2. **HVAC / building science learners** (~30%): understanding the relationship between U-factor and R-value, learning building physics, or doing their own Manual J calculation that requires U-factor inputs.

The article structures as: definition + R-value relationship (everyone), why U-factor matters (everyone), heat loss math (cohort 2 with cohort 1 satisfied by visualization), NFRC label reading (cohort 1 primary), recommended values by climate (everyone), what makes a window have low U-factor (cohort 1 buying decisions), windows in Manual J (cohort 2), cost/payback (cohort 1), edge cases and FAQ.

The article doesn't recommend specific brands. It teaches readers to read labels, then to make their own decisions. The 1,300/mo primary keyword is modest by itself but combined volume of the cluster is 6,610/mo and the article serves the entire building science fenestration cluster.

## Technical depth specification

### Section 1 — U-factor definition

U-factor (sometimes called U-value, especially in UK/SI usage):
- Definition: rate of heat transfer per unit area per unit temperature difference
- US units: BTU/(hr × ft² × °F)
- SI units: W/(m² × K)
- Unit conversion: 1 BTU/(hr·ft²·°F) ≈ 5.678 W/(m²·K)

A U-0.30 window in US units = 1.70 W/(m²·K) in SI.

R-value relationship:
- U = 1/R
- A U-0.30 window has R-3.3
- A U-1.04 window has R-0.96 (essentially R-1)

### Section 4 — NFRC label specifics

NFRC 100 procedure:
- U-factor measured at center-of-glass and at frame, then weighted by area
- Standard test conditions: -18°F outside, 70°F inside, 12.3 mph wind
- Reported to 2 decimal places

NFRC 200 procedure:
- SHGC measured under standard solar radiation
- VT measured for visible light only (380-780 nm)
- Both reported as fractions (0 to 1)

NFRC 400 procedure:
- Air leakage measured under ASTM E283
- Reported in cfm per linear foot of window edge at 1.57 lb/ft² pressure
- ENERGY STAR requires ≤0.3 cfm/ft²

### Section 6 — feature mechanics

**Second pane**: creates trapped gas layer. Heat transfer must cross gas (slower than glass). Most reduction.

**Low-E coating**: reflects long-wave IR. Glass is mostly transparent to long-wave IR otherwise. Reflecting it back into the room (or out of the room in summer) drops heat transfer through the radiative pathway.

**Argon (or krypton) gas**: denser than air, less convection between panes. Krypton works in narrower gaps (good for triple-pane).

**Warm-edge spacer**: traditional aluminum spacers conduct heat around the edge of the window. Foam or stainless steel "warm-edge" spacers reduce edge heat loss.

**Third pane**: another trapped gas layer. Marginal improvement compared to first additional pane.

### Section 7 — Manual J input requirements

Manual J wants per-window U-factor and SHGC, broken down by orientation (N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW). Some software accepts a single "window type" for the whole house; more accurate inputs make a meaningful difference.

LBNL Window 7 software computes whole-window performance from glass + spacer + frame specs. Available free for sizing-accuracy-critical projects.
