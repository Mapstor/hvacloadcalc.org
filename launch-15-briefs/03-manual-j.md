faq:
  - q: "Is Manual J required by code?"
    a: |
      In most US jurisdictions adopting the 2009 or later IECC, residential mechanical permits
      require a load calculation that complies with Manual J or equivalent. The specific
      enforcement varies by jurisdiction — some require a stamped calculation submitted with
      the permit, others accept a checkbox affirming compliance. Check your local building
      department for the exact submission requirement.

  - q: "How much does a professional Manual J cost?"
    a: |
      A Manual J performed by an independent third party typically runs $200-$600 in 2024 US
      pricing, depending on home complexity. HVAC contractors often include it in the system
      design when bidding a new installation. Beware of contractors who "do Manual J" by entering
      square footage into a rule-of-thumb tool and calling it a calculation — that is not a
      Manual J.

  - q: "Can I do my own Manual J calculation?"
    a: |
      Yes, but with caveats. ACCA-approved software ranges from $50 (RHVAC Lite) to $2,000+
      (Wrightsoft, Elite). The bigger limitation is gathering accurate inputs: insulation
      R-values, window U-factors, infiltration rates, and orientation matter, and getting these
      wrong throws off the result. A planning-grade DIY calculation is fine for estimation. A
      permit-ready calculation requires either approved software with verified inputs or a
      professional.

  - q: "What's the difference between Manual J, Manual S, Manual D, and Manual T?"
    a: |
      Manual J calculates the loads (how much heating and cooling the home needs). Manual S
      selects the equipment that meets those loads. Manual D designs the duct system to deliver
      the air. Manual T designs the air distribution at room registers and returns. The four
      manuals are a sequence: J first, then S, then D, then T.

  - q: "What is block load versus room-by-room?"
    a: |
      Block load calculates total whole-house heating and cooling needs as a single number.
      Room-by-room calculates the load for each individual room. Block load is enough for sizing
      the equipment. Room-by-room is required if you're also designing the duct system,
      because each duct branch needs to be sized to its room's load.

  - q: "How much oversizing is too much?"
    a: |
      Manual J explicitly allows oversizing tolerances: typically up to 15% above design heating
      load and up to 15-25% above design cooling load, depending on equipment type. Sizing more
      than 25% above the calculated cooling load is considered oversized and causes short cycling,
      poor humidity control, and wasted capacity. The exact tolerance depends on the equipment
      and is detailed in Manual S.

  - q: "Why is my contractor sizing based on square footage?"
    a: |
      Sizing by square footage (e.g., "1 ton per 600 square feet") is the historical rule of
      thumb. It often produces oversized systems because it ignores insulation level, window
      area and quality, orientation, infiltration, and internal gains. A properly performed
      Manual J for the same square footage frequently produces a smaller equipment recommendation
      than the rule of thumb, especially in newer or recently retrofitted homes.

  - q: "Does Manual J work for high-performance homes?"
    a: |
      Yes, but the default assumptions in Manual J can produce overstated loads for tight,
      well-insulated homes. ACCA-approved software allows you to enter measured infiltration
      rates (from a blower-door test) and verified insulation values. For very high-performance
      homes (passive house, deep retrofit), supplementing Manual J with detailed energy modeling
      software like BEopt or REM/Rate gives more accurate results.

  - q: "How long does a Manual J take?"
    a: |
      Performed in approved software with full home data on hand, a competent practitioner takes
      1-3 hours for a typical single-family home. Gathering inputs (insulation R-values, window
      specs, infiltration) often takes longer than the calculation itself. Field measurement and
      data collection can take 1-2 hours additionally.

  - q: "What inputs does a Manual J calculation need?"
    a: |
      The main inputs are: floor area and ceiling height per room or zone, wall construction and
      insulation R-value, ceiling and floor insulation R-values, window area, U-factor, SHGC and
      orientation per room, exterior door areas and types, infiltration rate (ACH50 if known),
      number of occupants and major appliances, ductwork location (conditioned or unconditioned),
      and 99% heating and 1% cooling design temperatures for the location.

## Search intent

Most searches for "manual j" come from one of three groups: (1) homeowners about to replace HVAC who heard the term from a contractor or online forum and want to understand what it is and whether they need one, (2) contractors and building permit staff confirming a definition or specific requirement, and (3) DIYers planning self-installs who need to determine equipment size.

Group 1 is the largest by far — the keyword cluster includes 1,300+ monthly searches for "what is manual j," "do i need a manual j," and "manual j cost." Their underlying question is "is this something I need to care about, and if so, what does it cost me?" They will leave the article either convinced they should request one from their contractor (good outcome) or confused about why it matters (bad outcome).

Group 2 is small but conversion-critical — they generate the inbound links from contractor forums and trade publications that this article needs to build authority. Their questions are specific (IECC code references, Manual S oversizing tolerances, block vs room-by-room).

Group 3 is the calculator funnel. They will read this article, then click through to the Manual J-style calculator. The article should give them enough understanding to use the calculator credibly without pretending DIY calculations substitute for professional ones in permit contexts.

The article serves all three by structuring as: definition first, then methodology overview, then decisions (when you need one, DIY vs professional, how to verify a contractor's calculation). It treats Manual J as a real engineering process — not a bureaucratic checkbox — without becoming a textbook.

## Technical depth specification

### Section 3 (How a Manual J works) — required technical content

**The core load calculation formula** (state in prose; do not show as equation block in the article — this is a homeowner-readable article):

The base formula behind every Manual J line item is: heat flow = U-value × area × temperature difference. U-value is the inverse of R-value. Area is the surface area (wall, window, ceiling). Temperature difference is between indoor design temperature and outdoor design temperature. For walls, ceilings, floors, doors, and windows, the calculation runs this formula per surface and per orientation, then sums.

**Additional load components Manual J handles**:
- Infiltration: ACH50 input (blower-door test) or estimated from construction tightness, converted to natural infiltration rate, then to heat loss
- Internal gains (cooling): occupant sensible/latent (per ASHRAE 55 standard occupant), lighting (W/sq ft default or specified), appliances (default + specified major)
- Solar heat gain (cooling): window SHGC × area × solar load factor (varies by orientation and time of day)
- Duct losses: load multiplier applied per duct location (conditioned space → 0%, vented attic → +20-25%)
- Ground-coupled losses (heating): basement and slab calculations use perimeter heat loss method

**Climate inputs** (cite ASHRAE 2021):
- 99% heating design temperature: temperature exceeded 99% of hours per year (≈ coldest 87 hours)
- 1% cooling design temperature: temperature exceeded 1% of hours per year (≈ hottest 87 hours)
- Mean coincident wet bulb: humidity at the cooling design hour
- Daily range: difference between daily high and low, used for cooling latent load

### Section 4 (Block load vs room-by-room) — technical detail

Block load assumes uniform distribution of heat loss across the conditioned envelope. Room-by-room assigns load to each room based on its individual walls, windows, ceiling, floor, infiltration share, and internal gains.

Block load sums approximately equal room-by-room totals for the same home (within calculation noise). The difference is what you can do with the data. Block load tells you what equipment to select. Room-by-room also tells you how much air each room needs, which is required for Manual D.

ACCA permits block load for equipment sizing only. Permit-grade duct design requires room-by-room.

### Section 7 (Oversizing) — required quantitative claims

LBNL and similar studies have found:
- Single-family residential HVAC oversized by an average of 30-50% above Manual J calculated load
- Oversizing is more common in larger homes and warmer climates
- Heat pump oversizing creates more severe comfort issues than gas furnace oversizing because of short cycling impact on dehumidification

DOE specifically states (paraphrase, cite): oversized equipment costs more upfront, cycles inefficiently, fails to control humidity, and shortens equipment life.

### Section 8 (When you need one) — required code language

IECC 2021 Section R403.7 states (paraphrase, cite): heating and cooling equipment shall be sized in accordance with ACCA Manual J or equivalent. Equipment selection in accordance with ACCA Manual S or equivalent.

This language appears in jurisdictions adopting IECC 2009 onward, which covers most US states by 2024. Local amendments vary; some states explicitly require submitted worksheets, others accept builder/contractor affirmation.
