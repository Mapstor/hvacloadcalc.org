faq:
  - q: "What size heat pump do I need for a 2000 square foot home?"
    a: |
      A 2,000 sq ft home typically needs a 2.5-3.5 ton heat pump (30,000-42,000 BTU/hr nominal
      cooling capacity), but this range is just a starting estimate. Climate, insulation quality,
      window area and quality, infiltration, ductwork condition, and orientation all shift the
      number by 30-50%. A Manual J load calculation gives the right answer for your specific home.

  - q: "Can I size a heat pump by square footage alone?"
    a: |
      Square footage is a starting point, not a final answer. Rules of thumb like "1 ton per 600
      square feet" ignore insulation, windows, air leakage, and climate — factors that easily
      shift the right size by 30-50%. Heat pumps in particular suffer from rule-of-thumb sizing
      because the heating capacity at design temperature differs significantly from the nominal
      cooling capacity, and the imbalance varies by climate.

  - q: "Should I size a heat pump to the heating load or cooling load?"
    a: |
      In cold climates, you typically size to a balance point — a temperature above which the
      heat pump handles 100% of the load and below which auxiliary heat assists. Sizing exactly
      to the heating load means a heat pump that's oversized for cooling. Sizing exactly to the
      cooling load means an aux-heavy installation. The middle ground depends on aux fuel cost,
      climate, and equipment type.

  - q: "What is the balance point in heat pump sizing?"
    a: |
      The balance point is the outdoor temperature at which the heat pump's heating capacity
      exactly equals the home's heating load. Above the balance point, the heat pump alone keeps
      up. Below it, auxiliary heat fills the gap. Sizing to a higher balance point reduces aux
      runtime but increases equipment cost and cooling oversizing risk.

  - q: "How is heat pump sizing different from AC sizing?"
    a: |
      AC sizing optimizes for the cooling design day only. Heat pump sizing must handle two
      different loads — heating and cooling — and they're rarely equal. The size that perfectly
      matches the cooling load may be too small for heating, especially in cold climates. The
      size that matches the heating load may be too large for cooling, causing short cycling
      and humidity problems in summer.

  - q: "What happens if my heat pump is oversized?"
    a: |
      Oversized heat pumps short cycle in cooling mode (similar to oversized AC), causing humidity
      control problems and reduced equipment life. In heating mode, oversizing has fewer downsides
      because aux heat is rarely engaged, but the higher purchase price and potential for cycling
      in mild weather are real costs. Variable-speed heat pumps tolerate oversizing better than
      single-stage units.

  - q: "What happens if my heat pump is undersized?"
    a: |
      Undersized heat pumps run constantly, struggle to maintain comfort on extreme days, and
      rely heavily on expensive auxiliary heat below the balance point. In cooling mode, an
      undersized unit can't keep up on the hottest days and indoor temperature rises. In heating,
      aux heat runtime becomes a significant portion of operating cost.

  - q: "Do mini-splits need different sizing?"
    a: |
      Mini-splits are still heat pumps and follow the same physics. The difference is per-zone
      sizing rather than whole-house. Multi-zone mini-split systems require accounting for
      simultaneous-vs-staggered loads (you rarely heat every zone at full capacity at the same
      time), which can allow for somewhat smaller condenser capacity than the sum of zone loads.

  - q: "How accurate is online heat pump sizing without a Manual J?"
    a: |
      Online calculators with reasonable inputs (square footage, climate zone, insulation level,
      window area) typically land within 20-30% of a proper Manual J. That accuracy is enough
      for planning purposes — choosing between 2-ton and 3-ton, evaluating contractor quotes —
      but not enough for actual permit-grade sizing. For the actual installation, get a Manual J
      from the contractor or hire it out independently.

  - q: "Do cold climate heat pumps need different sizing?"
    a: |
      Yes. Cold-climate heat pumps (CCASHP-rated) maintain capacity at much lower temperatures
      than standard heat pumps. This allows sizing for a lower balance point — sometimes the
      design temperature itself — which dramatically reduces aux heat runtime. The tradeoff is
      cost; cold-climate units are pricier than standard ones.

## Search intent

"Heat pump sizing" reflects three reader cohorts:

1. **Planning homeowner** (~60%): considering heat pump replacement or first install, wants a planning-grade answer to "what size do I need?" before talking to contractors. Will use the answer to evaluate quotes, not to actually install.

2. **DIY mini-split installer** (~25%): the explosion of 12k-36k BTU DIY-friendly mini-splits has created a substantial DIY sizing audience. They need accurate sizing because they're putting their own money on the line with no contractor backstop.

3. **Researcher / contractor verifier** (~15%): often homeowner with a Manual J in hand, comparing to what contractor proposed. Looking for sizing methodology context to push back on overspec'd quotes.

The article serves all three with: load explanation (everyone), rule-of-thumb critique (everyone), Manual J path (cohorts 1 and 3), balance-point methodology (cohort 3 mainly, with simplification for cohort 1), cold-climate considerations (cohorts 1 and 3 in cold zones), ducted-vs-ductless and mini-split context (cohort 2), oversize/undersize tradeoffs (everyone).

The "what size heat pump do i need" + square-footage queries (~3,900/mo combined) want concrete numbers. Article should give them but always paired with the "actual answer requires Manual J" caveat.

This article is the sizing methodology hub — child pages will cover heating-vs-cooling tradeoff, balance point design, cold climate, oversizing, undersizing, ducted-vs-ductless, by-square-footage. This article links to those for depth.

### H2 2 — How Heat Pumps Are Rated
**Target: 300 words**

- Nominal cooling capacity in BTU/hr or "tons" (1 ton = 12,000 BTU/hr)
- AHRI 210/240 rating conditions: 95°F outdoor / 80°F indoor for cooling, 47°F outdoor / 70°F indoor for heating
- Real-world capacity differs from nominal — drops with outdoor temperature in heating mode
- Modern ratings include capacity at additional outdoor temperatures (17°F, 5°F)
- HSPF2 (heating efficiency) and SEER2 (cooling efficiency) are seasonal averages
- Cold-climate certified products (NEEP CCASHP) report capacity at low temperatures
- Link to /heat-pump/performance/hspf2/
- Link to /manual-s/

### H2 3 — Heat Pump Sizing Is Different from AC Sizing
**Target: 300 words**

- AC sizing question: how much cooling does the home need on the 1% cooling design day?
- Heat pump sizing question: how much heating AND cooling does the home need? When loads conflict, which do you size to?
- Reference SVG: heating-vs-cooling-load-by-climate
- In hot climates (zone 1-2), cooling load dominates — size to cooling, aux handles minor heating
- In cold climates (zone 5-7), heating load dominates — sizing to cooling means substantial aux heat in winter
- In moderate climates (zone 3-4), the loads are similar but rarely equal
- The "split" of the home's loads determines which sizing strategy makes sense
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/heating-vs-cooling-load/

### H2 4 — Why Rule-of-Thumb Sizing Falls Short
**Target: 300 words**

- The classic: "1 ton per 600 sq ft" for cooling-dominant climates
- Variations: "1 ton per 500-700 sq ft" depending on region
- What it ignores: insulation, windows, air sealing, orientation, internal gains, ductwork condition
- Reference SVG: rule-of-thumb-vs-manual-j
- Typical result: rule of thumb produces 30-50% oversized vs Manual J for the same home
- Why this happens: rules of thumb were developed for 1970s-era construction; modern insulation and windows give very different loads
- For DIY/planning: square-footage estimates are fine as starting points
- For final install: get a Manual J
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/by-square-footage/
- Link to /tools/heat-pump-size-calculator/

### H2 5 — Why Heat Pump Sizing Matters
**Target: 300 words**

- Oversizing problems in cooling mode: short cycling, poor humidity control
- Oversizing problems in heating mode: less severe than cooling because heat pump cycles less aggressively
- Undersizing problems in cooling mode: can't keep up on hottest days, indoor temperature rises
- Undersizing problems in heating mode: aux heat runtime, expensive electric resistance use
- DOE position on right-sizing
- Lifespan impact: short-cycling shortens compressor life

### H2 6 — Balance Point Sizing Approach
**Target: 400 words (technical core)**

- Define: outdoor temperature at which heat pump capacity = home's heating load
- Reference SVG: capacity-vs-outdoor-temperature
- The construction: plot heat pump capacity vs outdoor temp (from manufacturer data), plot home load vs outdoor temp (from Manual J), find intersection
- Above balance point: heat pump alone keeps up
- Below balance point: aux heat assists
- Higher balance point = more aux runtime, smaller heat pump
- Lower balance point = less aux runtime, larger or cold-climate heat pump
- Cold-climate heat pumps shift the balance point dramatically lower (e.g., 8°F vs 25°F for the same home)
- Trade-offs in choosing balance point: equipment cost, aux fuel cost, cooling oversizing risk
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/balance-point-design/
- Link to /heat-pump/aux-heat/

### H2 7 — Cold Climate Considerations
**Target: 350 words**

- Design temperature: the 99% heating design temperature for the location, from ASHRAE Standard 169
- Below the design temperature, capacity is required but standard heat pumps may not deliver
- CCASHP (Cold Climate Air Source Heat Pump) certification: maintains rated capacity at low temperatures, demand-defrost, optimized refrigerant cycle
- Cold-climate sizing strategies (reference SVG: cold-climate-balance-point-strategy)
- The dual-fuel option: heat pump primary, gas/oil backup below balance point (common in cold climates with cheap gas)
- The all-electric option: heat pump + electric resistance aux (simpler but higher operating cost below balance point)
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/cold-climate/
- Link to /heat-pump/cold-climate/defrost-cycle/

### H2 8 — Oversizing vs Undersizing
**Target: 300 words**

- Reference SVG: oversize-undersize-tradeoffs
- Oversizing penalties: short cycling in cooling (humidity), wasted purchase price, possible short cycling in mild winter weather
- Manual S tolerances: 15-25% above Manual J cooling load is allowed; beyond that, oversized
- Undersizing penalties: aux heat runtime (cold), hot-day comfort failure (cooling), constant runtime
- The Goldilocks zone: typically right at the Manual J cooling load with appropriate balance point for heating
- Variable-speed (inverter) equipment tolerates oversizing better than single-stage
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/oversizing/
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/undersizing/

### H2 9 — System Type Variations
**Target: 300 words**

- Ducted central heat pump: single condenser, single air handler, distributes via duct system
- Sized for whole-house load
- Ductless mini-split (single zone): zone-by-zone sizing
- Multi-zone mini-split: single outdoor unit serving multiple indoor heads
- Multi-zone sizing nuance: simultaneous-vs-staggered loads allow somewhat smaller outdoor unit
- Combined diversity factor: typically 0.7-0.85 for multi-zone (sum of indoor zones × diversity = required outdoor capacity)
- Don't oversize outdoor unit on multi-zone — limits modulation range
- Link to /heat-pump/sizing/ducted-vs-ductless/

## Technical depth specification

### Section 2 — equipment ratings detail

AHRI 210/240 reports:
- **Nominal cooling capacity**: BTU/hr at 95°F outdoor dry bulb, 80°F indoor dry bulb, 67°F indoor wet bulb
- **Nominal heating capacity (high temp)**: BTU/hr at 47°F outdoor, 70°F indoor
- **Heating capacity at 17°F**: BTU/hr at 17°F outdoor, 70°F indoor
- **Heating capacity at 5°F** (for cold-climate certified): BTU/hr at 5°F outdoor, 70°F indoor
- **SEER2**: seasonal energy efficiency ratio (cooling), BTU per Wh, 1.0 SEER2 difference is meaningful
- **HSPF2**: heating seasonal performance factor, BTU per Wh, 1.0 HSPF2 difference is meaningful

Common capacity drop: a heat pump with 36,000 BTU/hr at 47°F may deliver only 22,000 BTU/hr at 17°F (61% of rated) and 12,000 BTU/hr at 5°F (33% of rated). A cold-climate certified unit might deliver 30,000 at 17°F and 25,000 at 5°F (83% and 69% respectively).

### Section 6 — balance point calculation

For a home with design heating load 45,000 BTU/hr at 5°F design temperature, indoor at 70°F:

1. Plot home load: 0 BTU/hr at 70°F, rising linearly to 45,000 at 5°F
2. Plot heat pump capacity: e.g., 36,000 at 47°F, 22,000 at 17°F, 12,000 at 5°F → curve
3. Intersection (balance point): where these two curves meet
4. For standard heat pump above: balance point around 28°F (heat pump delivers ~22,000 BTU/hr = home load at 28°F)
5. Above 28°F: heat pump alone
6. Below 28°F: aux fills gap (e.g., at 5°F, aux must provide 45,000 - 12,000 = 33,000 BTU/hr)

A cold-climate heat pump with 30,000 at 17°F shifts the same calculation:
- Balance point much lower (maybe 12-15°F)
- Aux runtime sharply reduced

The choice: sizing to a lower balance point means cold-climate equipment cost + possibly slight cooling oversizing.

### Section 7 — design temperature look-up

ASHRAE Standard 169 publishes 99% heating design temperatures for 5,000+ US locations. The 99% means the outdoor temperature is at or above this value 99% of hours in a typical year (about 87 hours per year are colder).

Sample values:
- Miami, FL: 47°F
- Phoenix, AZ: 33°F
- Atlanta, GA: 22°F
- Denver, CO: 4°F
- Chicago, IL: -2°F
- Minneapolis, MN: -11°F
- Anchorage, AK: -19°F

Local code may specify a different design temperature; check with the local building department.

## Prose anchors (voice calibration)

### Opening hook (use exactly, do not rewrite)

> Sizing a heat pump is harder than sizing an air conditioner, and most contractors don't do it right. The difference is structural: an AC only has to handle the cooling load. The size that perfectly matches one load almost certainly mismatches the other.

>

### Anchor paragraph — H2 section 6 (Balance point sizing approach), opening (use exactly)

> The balance point is the outdoor temperature at which your heat pump's heating capacity exactly equals your home's heating load. Above it, the heat pump alone keeps you warm. Below it, auxiliary heat fills the gap. This is the single most important concept in heat pump sizing, because it determines both how much aux heat you'll run and how much you'll spend on the equipment itself. A higher balance point — say 35°F — means a smaller, cheaper heat pump that needs aux help any time it's below freezing. A lower balance point — say 10°F — means a larger, pricier heat pump (often a cold-climate certified one) that rarely needs aux. Neither is universally right. The choice depends on the cost of aux fuel where you live, how often it actually gets cold, and your budget.
