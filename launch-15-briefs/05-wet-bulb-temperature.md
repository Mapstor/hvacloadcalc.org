faq:
  - q: "Is wet bulb temperature the same as 'feels like' temperature?"
    a: |
      No. "Feels like" temperature is the heat index, calculated from dry bulb temperature and
      relative humidity using a formula developed by NOAA to estimate how the temperature feels
      to a typical person in shade. Wet bulb temperature is a physical measurement of the
      lowest temperature that air can reach by evaporation. The two are related but not the
      same. A 90°F dry bulb at 50% RH gives a wet bulb of 78°F and a heat index of 106°F.

  - q: "What's a dangerous wet bulb temperature?"
    a: |
      For healthy adults at rest in shade with airflow, sustained wet bulb temperatures above
      85°F begin to stress thermoregulation. Above 90°F, even healthy adults at rest face
      heat stroke risk. The widely-cited 95°F (35°C) threshold is the theoretical upper limit
      where human skin can no longer cool by evaporation. Recent research (Penn State, 2022)
      suggests the practical survivability threshold is closer to 88°F (31°C) wet bulb for
      most healthy adults.

  - q: "How do you measure wet bulb temperature?"
    a: |
      Traditionally with a sling psychrometer — two thermometers mounted together, one with
      a wet cotton wick over its bulb, slung through the air for 30 seconds, then both read
      quickly. Modern instruments use fan-aspirated psychrometers or solid-state sensors with
      built-in calculations from dry bulb and relative humidity. The Stull formula gives a
      good approximation from temperature and humidity readings.

  - q: "What's the difference between wet bulb and dew point?"
    a: |
      Dew point is the temperature at which water vapor in the air starts to condense. Wet
      bulb is the lowest temperature air can reach by evaporative cooling. Wet bulb is always
      between dry bulb and dew point. At 100% relative humidity, dry bulb, wet bulb, and dew
      point are all equal. As humidity drops, the three diverge — wet bulb stays closer to
      dry bulb than dew point does.

  - q: "What is WBGT?"
    a: |
      Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) is a heat-stress index used in occupational health,
      sports, and military training. It combines wet bulb temperature, dry bulb temperature,
      and globe temperature (which captures solar radiation) into a single number. NIOSH and
      OSHA use WBGT thresholds to set work-rest cycles in hot environments.

  - q: "Why is wet bulb important for HVAC?"
    a: |
      Wet bulb is the design condition for the cooling tower and evaporative cooling equipment
      industries — it's the theoretical lower limit those systems can reach. For air
      conditioning, the mean coincident wet bulb (the typical wet bulb that occurs at the
      cooling design dry bulb) determines the latent (dehumidification) portion of the cooling
      load. ASHRAE design data publishes 1% dry bulb design conditions with their mean
      coincident wet bulb.

  - q: "Can wet bulb temperature exceed dry bulb temperature?"
    a: |
      No. Wet bulb temperature is always less than or equal to dry bulb temperature. The two
      are equal only at 100% relative humidity. Whenever evaporation is possible (humidity
      below 100%), wet bulb is lower than dry bulb. The difference between dry bulb and wet
      bulb is called the wet bulb depression.

  - q: "How is wet bulb temperature different from absolute humidity?"
    a: |
      Wet bulb is a temperature measurement that captures both temperature and moisture in
      one number, expressed in degrees. Absolute humidity is the mass of water vapor per unit
      volume of air, expressed in grams per cubic meter or grains per pound of dry air. They
      are related (both depend on temperature and moisture) but they are different physical
      quantities with different uses.

  - q: "What is the highest wet bulb temperature ever recorded?"
    a: |
      Reliable peak wet bulb temperatures of 35°C (95°F) have been measured in the Persian
      Gulf, particularly at sites in coastal Iran, the UAE, and Saudi Arabia. South Asia
      (Pakistan, India) has measured 33-34°C (91-93°F) wet bulb during pre-monsoon heat waves.
      Sustained wet bulb above 31°C (88°F) is rare but increasing in frequency with climate
      change.

  - q: "Where can I find current wet bulb temperatures?"
    a: |
      NOAA's National Weather Service publishes current observations including wet bulb at
      most reporting stations. Apps like Ambient Weather, Weather Underground, and Tempest
      include wet bulb on their station detail pages. For historical and climate-design wet
      bulb data, ASHRAE Standard 169 publishes mean coincident wet bulb values for thousands
      of locations.

### H2 6 — How to Calculate Wet Bulb Temperature
**Target: 350 words**

- Exact calculation requires iterative solving of psychrometric equations
- Approximation: Stull (2011) formula — empirical, good within 0.3°C for typical conditions
- Show the formula in a code block:
```
Tw ≈ T × atan(0.151977 × √(RH + 8.313659))
    + atan(T + RH) − atan(RH − 1.676331)
    + 0.00391838 × RH^1.5 × atan(0.023101 × RH)
    − 4.686035

Where:
  Tw = wet bulb temperature (°C)
  T  = dry bulb temperature (°C)
  RH = relative humidity (%)
```
- Worked example: 30°C dry bulb, 60% RH → ~23.4°C wet bulb
- For US units: convert °F to °C first, then apply formula, then convert back
- The site's wet bulb calculator runs this formula automatically

## Technical depth specification

### Section 1 — definitional precision

The wet bulb temperature is defined under the condition of steady-state heat and mass transfer between a wet surface and the air, with no external heat source. In practice:
- A wick wet with pure water covers a thermometer bulb
- Air passes over the wick at sufficient velocity (>2 m/s for accurate readings)
- Water evaporates from the wick, removing heat from the bulb
- The bulb temperature drops until heat lost to evaporation equals heat gained from the surrounding air by conduction and convection
- This equilibrium temperature is the wet bulb

If air is dry, evaporation is rapid, equilibrium is at a lower temperature → wet bulb is much lower than dry bulb.
If air is saturated (100% RH), no evaporation occurs, no cooling → wet bulb = dry bulb.

### Section 5 — psychrometric chart relationships

For any moist air state, exactly two independent properties determine all others. Common pairs:
- Dry bulb + relative humidity (most common)
- Dry bulb + wet bulb
- Dry bulb + dew point
- Dry bulb + humidity ratio

Once two are known, all of these are determined:
- Specific volume (m³/kg dry air)
- Enthalpy (kJ/kg dry air)
- Humidity ratio (kg water vapor / kg dry air)
- Vapor pressure
- Degree of saturation

The psychrometric chart visualizes this. ASHRAE publishes charts at standard barometric pressure (101.325 kPa). At different elevations or pressures, modified charts are needed.

Lines of constant wet bulb are nearly (but not exactly) parallel to lines of constant enthalpy. The slight divergence comes from the small enthalpy contribution of the water added to the air during the wet bulb measurement process.

### Section 6 — Stull formula caveats

Stull's 2011 formula is accurate within:
- 0.3°C for 95% of conditions with 5% < RH ≤ 99% and -20°C ≤ T ≤ 50°C
- Accuracy degrades at very low humidity (<5% RH) and very low temperatures (<-20°C)

For HVAC engineering precision, use full psychrometric equations or ASHRAE-validated software, not Stull's approximation. For weather, education, and general purposes, Stull is excellent.

### Section 8 — WBGT calculation detail

Natural wet bulb (Tw,n) in WBGT is measured with no aspiration — different from the standard wet bulb measurement. It captures the cooling effect from natural air movement only, which is more representative of human heat exposure.

OSHA does not have a federal WBGT-based heat stress standard as of 2024, though specific industries (military, athletics) do. State-level standards exist in California (Title 8 Section 3395) and Washington.

### Section 9 — survivability physics

The 35°C wet bulb limit assumes:
- Healthy adult at rest
- No exertion
- Shade
- Some air movement
- Adequate hydration

Under these conditions, sweat evaporates fast enough to cool the body up to ~35°C wet bulb. Above that, the temperature gradient from skin (~35°C) to environment is zero or reversed; heat cannot leave the body.

Vecellio et al. (2022) found the actual physiological limit is closer to 30-31°C wet bulb because real humans are not at rest with perfect cooling conditions. The 35°C number is an idealized upper bound.
